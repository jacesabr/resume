Generative Adversarial Networks

Two neural networks locked in a minimax game — a Generator that forges data and a Discriminator that detects forgeries — training each other until the Generator produces samples indistinguishable from real data.

Mathematical Foundation

The GAN framework is formulated as a two-player minimax game between a Generator \(G\) and a Discriminator \(D\). The Generator maps latent noise \(z \sim p_z(z)\) to data space, while the Discriminator outputs the probability that a sample came from the real data distribution rather than \(G\).

Minimax Objective (Value Function)
\[\min_G \max_D V(D,G) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_{\text{data}}(x)}[\log D(x)] + \mathbb{E}_{z \sim p_z(z)}[\log(1 - D(G(z)))]\]

The Discriminator maximizes the probability of correctly classifying real vs. fake. The Generator minimizes the Discriminator's ability to distinguish.

Discriminator Loss
\[\mathcal{L}_D = -\mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_{\text{data}}}[\log D(x)] - \mathbb{E}_{z \sim p_z}[\log(1 - D(G(z)))]\]

Binary cross-entropy: push \(D(x) \to 1\) for real, \(D(G(z)) \to 0\) for fake.

Generator Loss
\[\mathcal{L}_G = -\mathbb{E}_{z \sim p_z}[\log D(G(z))]\]

Non-saturating form: maximize \(\log D(G(z))\) instead of minimizing \(\log(1 - D(G(z)))\) for stronger early gradients.

Optimal Discriminator
\[D^*_G(x) = \frac{p_{\text{data}}(x)}{p_{\text{data}}(x) + p_g(x)}\]

At optimality, the global minimum of \(V(D^*, G)\) is achieved if and only if \(p_g = p_{\text{data}}\), yielding \(D^*(x) = \frac{1}{2}\) everywhere.

Global Minimum & Jensen-Shannon Divergence
\[C(G) = -\log 4 + 2 \cdot \text{JSD}(p_{\text{data}} \| p_g)\]

The minimax game implicitly minimizes the Jensen-Shannon Divergence between the real and generated distributions. The global minimum \(-\log 4\) is reached when \(p_g = p_{\text{data}}\).

The Adversarial Game

The Counterfeiter vs. Detective Analogy. Imagine a counterfeiter (Generator) trying to produce fake currency, and a detective (Discriminator) trying to tell real bills from fakes. Each time the detective catches a forgery, the counterfeiter learns what went wrong and improves. Each time the counterfeiter succeeds, the detective sharpens their eye. Over thousands of rounds, the counterfeiter becomes so skilled that even the detective cannot distinguish fake from real.

Nash Equilibrium. The theoretical convergence point is a Nash equilibrium where the Generator perfectly replicates \(p_{\text{data}}\) and the Discriminator outputs \(\frac{1}{2}\) for every sample — it genuinely cannot tell the difference. In practice, GANs rarely reach true equilibrium; instead they oscillate near it.

Training Dynamics

Alternating optimization: Train D for \(k\) steps (typically \(k=1\)), then train G for 1 step. D must stay ahead of G so its gradients remain informative, but not so far ahead that gradients vanish.

Gradient Flow

G never sees real data directly. Its only learning signal comes through backpropagation from D's output. If D becomes too strong too early, gradients saturate and G stops learning. If D is too weak, G receives no useful signal.

Convergence Challenges

Non-convex, non-cooperative game. Standard gradient descent guarantees do not apply. Training can oscillate, diverge, or collapse. Careful hyperparameter tuning and architectural choices are essential.

Measuring Progress

Loss is not a good metric. G and D losses oscillate by design. Use FID (Frechet Inception Distance) or IS (Inception Score) to evaluate sample quality. Visual inspection remains important.

Architecture Overview

The GAN consists of two competing neural networks trained simultaneously. The Generator takes random noise from a latent space and transforms it into synthetic data samples. The Discriminator receives both real data and generated samples, outputting a probability that the input is real.

Generator Network

Input: Latent vector \(z \in \mathbb{R}^{d_z}\) sampled from \(\mathcal{N}(0, I)\) or \(\text{Uniform}(-1, 1)\).
Architecture: Fully connected or transposed convolutional layers. Maps low-dimensional noise to high-dimensional data space.
Output: Synthetic sample \(G(z) \in \mathbb{R}^{d_x}\) matching the shape of real data.
Activations: ReLU/LeakyReLU internally, Tanh or Sigmoid at output.

Discriminator Network

Input: Data sample \(x\) (either real or generated).
Architecture: Convolutional or fully connected layers. Progressively reduces spatial dimensions.
Output: Scalar probability \(D(x) \in [0, 1]\).
Activations: LeakyReLU internally, Sigmoid at output. Batch normalization used cautiously (not in D's first/last layers).

Latent Space z

The latent space is the compressed, continuous representation from which the Generator draws its creative material. Dimensionality typically ranges from 64 to 512. Interpolation between two latent vectors produces smooth transitions in generated output — walking through latent space reveals the learned data manifold. Disentanglement (e.g., in StyleGAN) allows individual latent dimensions to control specific attributes like pose, lighting, or expression.

Training Loop (Pseudocode)
for each training iteration:
  # --- Train Discriminator ---
  Sample real batch x ~ p_data
  Sample noise z ~ p_z
  fake = G(z)
  L_D = -mean(log(D(x))) - mean(log(1 - D(fake)))
  Update D to minimize L_D

  # --- Train Generator ---
  Sample noise z ~ p_z
  fake = G(z)
  L_G = -mean(log(D(fake)))
  Update G to minimize L_G

Key Concepts

Latent Space

A low-dimensional continuous vector space from which the Generator samples input noise. Smooth interpolation in this space yields meaningful transitions in output. Typical dimensionality: 100-512. The Generator learns to map this space onto the data manifold.

Mode Collapse

The Generator discovers a few outputs that consistently fool the Discriminator and stops exploring the full distribution. All outputs converge to a small set of modes. Mitigation: minibatch discrimination, unrolled GANs, feature matching, or Wasserstein loss.

Training Instability

GAN training is a saddle-point optimization problem, not a simple minimization. Gradients can vanish (D too strong), oscillate (D and G alternate dominance), or diverge entirely. Solutions: spectral normalization, two-timescale update rules (TTUR), careful learning rate scheduling.

Wasserstein Distance

Also called Earth Mover's Distance. Measures the minimum "work" to transform one distribution into another: \(W(p_r, p_g) = \inf_{\gamma \in \Pi} \mathbb{E}[\|x - y\|]\). Provides meaningful gradients even when distributions have non-overlapping support — unlike JS divergence.

Spectral Normalization

Constrains the Lipschitz constant of the Discriminator by normalizing each weight matrix by its largest singular value: \(\bar{W} = W / \sigma(W)\). Stabilizes training without heavy regularization. Applied per-layer, computationally cheap via power iteration.

Progressive Growing

Introduced in ProGAN: start training at low resolution (4x4) and progressively add layers to both G and D to reach higher resolutions (up to 1024x1024). Stabilizes training by allowing the networks to learn coarse structure first, then refine fine details.

Code Example 1: DCGAN on MNIST

A Deep Convolutional GAN using transposed convolutions in the Generator and strided convolutions in the Discriminator. Trained on MNIST handwritten digits (28x28 grayscale).

PyTorch — DCGAN 
import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.optim as optim
from torchvision import datasets, transforms
from torch.utils.data import DataLoader

# ── Hyperparameters ──────────────────────────────────
latent_dim = 100
batch_size = 128
lr = 0.0002
betas = (0.5, 0.999)
epochs = 50
device = torch.device("cuda" if torch.cuda.is_available() else "cpu")

# ── Data ─────────────────────────────────────────────
transform = transforms.Compose([
    transforms.Resize(32),
    transforms.ToTensor(),
    transforms.Normalize([0.5], [0.5])  # Scale to [-1, 1]
])
dataset = datasets.MNIST(root="./data", train=True, transform=transform, download=True)
loader = DataLoader(dataset, batch_size=batch_size, shuffle=True, drop_last=True)


class Generator(nn.Module):
    """Maps latent z (100,) → image (1, 32, 32) via transposed convolutions."""
    def __init__(self):
        super().__init__()
        self.net = nn.Sequential(
            # z → (256, 4, 4)
            nn.ConvTranspose2d(latent_dim, 256, 4, 1, 0, bias=False),
            nn.BatchNorm2d(256),
            nn.ReLU(True),
            # → (128, 8, 8)
            nn.ConvTranspose2d(256, 128, 4, 2, 1, bias=False),
            nn.BatchNorm2d(128),
            nn.ReLU(True),
            # → (64, 16, 16)
            nn.ConvTranspose2d(128, 64, 4, 2, 1, bias=False),
            nn.BatchNorm2d(64),
            nn.ReLU(True),
            # → (1, 32, 32)
            nn.ConvTranspose2d(64, 1, 4, 2, 1, bias=False),
            nn.Tanh()
        )

    def forward(self, z):
        return self.net(z.view(-1, latent_dim, 1, 1))


class Discriminator(nn.Module):
    """Classifies image (1, 32, 32) → real/fake probability."""
    def __init__(self):
        super().__init__()
        self.net = nn.Sequential(
            # (1, 32, 32) → (64, 16, 16)
            nn.Conv2d(1, 64, 4, 2, 1, bias=False),
            nn.LeakyReLU(0.2, inplace=True),
            # → (128, 8, 8)
            nn.Conv2d(64, 128, 4, 2, 1, bias=False),
            nn.BatchNorm2d(128),
            nn.LeakyReLU(0.2, inplace=True),
            # → (256, 4, 4)
            nn.Conv2d(128, 256, 4, 2, 1, bias=False),
            nn.BatchNorm2d(256),
            nn.LeakyReLU(0.2, inplace=True),
            # → (1, 1, 1)
            nn.Conv2d(256, 1, 4, 1, 0, bias=False),
            nn.Sigmoid()
        )

    def forward(self, x):
        return self.net(x).view(-1)


# ── Weight Initialization (DCGAN paper) ──────────────
def weights_init(m):
    if isinstance(m, (nn.Conv2d, nn.ConvTranspose2d)):
        nn.init.normal_(m.weight, 0.0, 0.02)
    elif isinstance(m, nn.BatchNorm2d):
        nn.init.normal_(m.weight, 1.0, 0.02)
        nn.init.zeros_(m.bias)


# ── Initialize ───────────────────────────────────────
G = Generator().to(device)
D = Discriminator().to(device)
G.apply(weights_init)
D.apply(weights_init)

opt_G = optim.Adam(G.parameters(), lr=lr, betas=betas)
opt_D = optim.Adam(D.parameters(), lr=lr, betas=betas)
criterion = nn.BCELoss()

# ── Training Loop ────────────────────────────────────
for epoch in range(epochs):
    for i, (real_imgs, _) in enumerate(loader):
        real_imgs = real_imgs.to(device)
        bs = real_imgs.size(0)

        real_labels = torch.ones(bs, device=device)
        fake_labels = torch.zeros(bs, device=device)

        # ── Train Discriminator ──────────────────────
        z = torch.randn(bs, latent_dim, device=device)
        fake_imgs = G(z).detach()

        loss_D_real = criterion(D(real_imgs), real_labels)
        loss_D_fake = criterion(D(fake_imgs), fake_labels)
        loss_D = loss_D_real + loss_D_fake

        opt_D.zero_grad()
        loss_D.backward()
        opt_D.step()

        # ── Train Generator ──────────────────────────
        z = torch.randn(bs, latent_dim, device=device)
        fake_imgs = G(z)
        loss_G = criterion(D(fake_imgs), real_labels)  # Fool D

        opt_G.zero_grad()
        loss_G.backward()
        opt_G.step()

    print(f"Epoch [{epoch+1}/{epochs}]  D_loss: {loss_D.item():.4f}  G_loss: {loss_G.item():.4f}")

Code Example 2: Conditional GAN (cGAN)

A Conditional GAN conditions both G and D on class labels, enabling controlled generation. Instead of random samples, you can request "generate a 7" or "generate a 3". The label is embedded and concatenated with the latent vector (for G) or with the image features (for D).

PyTorch — Conditional GAN 
import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.optim as optim
from torchvision import datasets, transforms
from torch.utils.data import DataLoader

latent_dim = 100
num_classes = 10
img_shape = 784    # 28 * 28 flattened
embed_dim = 50
batch_size = 128
lr = 0.0002
epochs = 100
device = torch.device("cuda" if torch.cuda.is_available() else "cpu")


class ConditionalGenerator(nn.Module):
    """Takes latent z + class label → generates image."""
    def __init__(self):
        super().__init__()
        self.label_embed = nn.Embedding(num_classes, embed_dim)
        self.net = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(latent_dim + embed_dim, 256),
            nn.LeakyReLU(0.2),
            nn.BatchNorm1d(256),
            nn.Linear(256, 512),
            nn.LeakyReLU(0.2),
            nn.BatchNorm1d(512),
            nn.Linear(512, 1024),
            nn.LeakyReLU(0.2),
            nn.BatchNorm1d(1024),
            nn.Linear(1024, img_shape),
            nn.Tanh()
        )

    def forward(self, z, labels):
        label_emb = self.label_embed(labels)
        x = torch.cat([z, label_emb], dim=1)
        return self.net(x)


class ConditionalDiscriminator(nn.Module):
    """Takes image + class label → real/fake."""
    def __init__(self):
        super().__init__()
        self.label_embed = nn.Embedding(num_classes, embed_dim)
        self.net = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(img_shape + embed_dim, 512),
            nn.LeakyReLU(0.2),
            nn.Dropout(0.3),
            nn.Linear(512, 256),
            nn.LeakyReLU(0.2),
            nn.Dropout(0.3),
            nn.Linear(256, 1),
            nn.Sigmoid()
        )

    def forward(self, img, labels):
        label_emb = self.label_embed(labels)
        x = torch.cat([img, label_emb], dim=1)
        return self.net(x).squeeze()


# ── Data ─────────────────────────────────────────────
transform = transforms.Compose([
    transforms.ToTensor(),
    transforms.Normalize([0.5], [0.5])
])
dataset = datasets.MNIST("./data", train=True, transform=transform, download=True)
loader = DataLoader(dataset, batch_size=batch_size, shuffle=True, drop_last=True)

# ── Initialize ───────────────────────────────────────
G = ConditionalGenerator().to(device)
D = ConditionalDiscriminator().to(device)
opt_G = optim.Adam(G.parameters(), lr=lr, betas=(0.5, 0.999))
opt_D = optim.Adam(D.parameters(), lr=lr, betas=(0.5, 0.999))
criterion = nn.BCELoss()

# ── Training Loop ────────────────────────────────────
for epoch in range(epochs):
    for real_imgs, labels in loader:
        bs = real_imgs.size(0)
        real_imgs = real_imgs.view(bs, -1).to(device)
        labels = labels.to(device)

        real_t = torch.ones(bs, device=device)
        fake_t = torch.zeros(bs, device=device)

        # ── Discriminator ────────────────────────────
        z = torch.randn(bs, latent_dim, device=device)
        fake_imgs = G(z, labels).detach()

        loss_D = criterion(D(real_imgs, labels), real_t) \
               + criterion(D(fake_imgs, labels), fake_t)

        opt_D.zero_grad()
        loss_D.backward()
        opt_D.step()

        # ── Generator ────────────────────────────────
        z = torch.randn(bs, latent_dim, device=device)
        fake_imgs = G(z, labels)
        loss_G = criterion(D(fake_imgs, labels), real_t)

        opt_G.zero_grad()
        loss_G.backward()
        opt_G.step()

    print(f"Epoch {epoch+1}/{epochs}  D: {loss_D.item():.4f}  G: {loss_G.item():.4f}")

# ── Generate specific digits ─────────────────────────
with torch.no_grad():
    z = torch.randn(10, latent_dim, device=device)
    labels = torch.arange(10, device=device)  # One of each digit
    generated = G(z, labels).view(10, 1, 28, 28)
    print(f"Generated conditioned samples: {generated.shape}")

Code Example 3: WGAN-GP (Wasserstein GAN with Gradient Penalty)

The Wasserstein GAN replaces the JS divergence with the Earth Mover's Distance, yielding more stable training and a meaningful loss curve. The gradient penalty enforces the Lipschitz constraint on the Critic (no longer a "Discriminator" — outputs an unbounded score, not a probability). No sigmoid, no BCE loss, no batch norm in the Critic.

PyTorch — WGAN-GP 
import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.optim as optim
from torchvision import datasets, transforms
from torch.utils.data import DataLoader
import torch.autograd as autograd

latent_dim = 128
n_critic = 5         # Train critic 5x per generator step
lambda_gp = 10       # Gradient penalty coefficient
batch_size = 64
lr = 1e-4
epochs = 100
device = torch.device("cuda" if torch.cuda.is_available() else "cpu")


class Generator(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self):
        super().__init__()
        self.net = nn.Sequential(
            nn.ConvTranspose2d(latent_dim, 256, 4, 1, 0),
            nn.BatchNorm2d(256), nn.ReLU(True),
            nn.ConvTranspose2d(256, 128, 4, 2, 1),
            nn.BatchNorm2d(128), nn.ReLU(True),
            nn.ConvTranspose2d(128, 64, 4, 2, 1),
            nn.BatchNorm2d(64), nn.ReLU(True),
            nn.ConvTranspose2d(64, 1, 4, 2, 1),
            nn.Tanh()
        )

    def forward(self, z):
        return self.net(z.view(-1, latent_dim, 1, 1))


class Critic(nn.Module):
    """No sigmoid — outputs unbounded Wasserstein score.
    No batch norm — use layer norm or nothing."""
    def __init__(self):
        super().__init__()
        self.net = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Conv2d(1, 64, 4, 2, 1),
            nn.LeakyReLU(0.2, inplace=True),
            nn.Conv2d(64, 128, 4, 2, 1),
            nn.LayerNorm([128, 8, 8]),
            nn.LeakyReLU(0.2, inplace=True),
            nn.Conv2d(128, 256, 4, 2, 1),
            nn.LayerNorm([256, 4, 4]),
            nn.LeakyReLU(0.2, inplace=True),
            nn.Conv2d(256, 1, 4, 1, 0)
        )

    def forward(self, x):
        return self.net(x).view(-1)


def gradient_penalty(critic, real, fake, device):
    """Enforces 1-Lipschitz by penalizing gradient norm on interpolated samples."""
    alpha = torch.rand(real.size(0), 1, 1, 1, device=device)
    interpolated = (alpha * real + (1 - alpha) * fake).requires_grad_(True)

    scores = critic(interpolated)
    gradients = autograd.grad(
        outputs=scores,
        inputs=interpolated,
        grad_outputs=torch.ones_like(scores),
        create_graph=True,
        retain_graph=True
    )[0]

    gradients = gradients.view(gradients.size(0), -1)
    gp = ((gradients.norm(2, dim=1) - 1) ** 2).mean()
    return gp


# ── Data ─────────────────────────────────────────────
transform = transforms.Compose([
    transforms.Resize(32),
    transforms.ToTensor(),
    transforms.Normalize([0.5], [0.5])
])
dataset = datasets.MNIST("./data", train=True, transform=transform, download=True)
loader = DataLoader(dataset, batch_size=batch_size, shuffle=True, drop_last=True)

# ── Initialize ───────────────────────────────────────
G = Generator().to(device)
C = Critic().to(device)
opt_G = optim.Adam(G.parameters(), lr=lr, betas=(0.0, 0.9))
opt_C = optim.Adam(C.parameters(), lr=lr, betas=(0.0, 0.9))

# ── Training Loop ────────────────────────────────────
for epoch in range(epochs):
    for i, (real_imgs, _) in enumerate(loader):
        real_imgs = real_imgs.to(device)
        bs = real_imgs.size(0)

        # ── Train Critic (n_critic times) ────────────
        for _ in range(n_critic):
            z = torch.randn(bs, latent_dim, device=device)
            fake_imgs = G(z).detach()

            # Wasserstein loss: maximize E[C(real)] - E[C(fake)]
            loss_C = C(fake_imgs).mean() - C(real_imgs).mean()
            gp = gradient_penalty(C, real_imgs, fake_imgs, device)
            loss_C_total = loss_C + lambda_gp * gp

            opt_C.zero_grad()
            loss_C_total.backward()
            opt_C.step()

        # ── Train Generator ──────────────────────────
        z = torch.randn(bs, latent_dim, device=device)
        fake_imgs = G(z)
        loss_G = -C(fake_imgs).mean()  # Maximize critic score on fakes

        opt_G.zero_grad()
        loss_G.backward()
        opt_G.step()

    # Wasserstein distance estimate (negative critic loss)
    w_dist = -loss_C.item()
    print(f"Epoch {epoch+1}/{epochs}  W_dist: {w_dist:.4f}  G_loss: {loss_G.item():.4f}")

GAN Variants

DCGAN (2015)

Deep Convolutional GAN. Replaced fully connected layers with strided convolutions in D and transposed convolutions in G. Established architectural guidelines: batch norm, LeakyReLU in D, ReLU in G, no pooling layers. First GAN to reliably generate coherent images at 64x64.

WGAN / WGAN-GP (2017)

Wasserstein GAN. Replaced JS divergence with Earth Mover's Distance. Critic (not discriminator) outputs unbounded score. WGAN-GP adds gradient penalty for Lipschitz constraint instead of weight clipping. Result: stable training, meaningful loss curves, reduced mode collapse.

StyleGAN / StyleGAN2 (2018-2020)

Style-based Generator. Mapping network transforms z into intermediate style space W. Adaptive instance normalization (AdaIN) injects style at each layer. Progressive growing from 4x4 to 1024x1024. State-of-the-art face generation. StyleGAN2 removes artifacts via weight demodulation.

CycleGAN (2017)

Unpaired image-to-image translation. Two generators (G: A→B, F: B→A) with cycle consistency loss: F(G(a)) ≈ a. No paired training data needed. Applications: horse↔zebra, summer↔winter, photo↔painting. Key innovation: cycle consistency loss.

Pix2Pix (2016)

Paired image-to-image translation. Conditional GAN with U-Net generator and PatchGAN discriminator. Requires aligned pairs (e.g., sketch→photo, segmentation→image). L1 reconstruction loss + adversarial loss. PatchGAN classifies NxN patches instead of whole image.

ProGAN (2017)

Progressive Growing of GANs. Starts training at 4x4, progressively doubles resolution by adding layers with smooth fade-in. Generates up to 1024x1024 faces. Key insight: learning coarse structure first makes high-resolution synthesis tractable. Precursor to StyleGAN.

When to Use / When to Avoid

When to Use GANs

Photorealistic image synthesis — faces, rooms, objects, art

Image-to-image translation — sketch-to-photo, style transfer, colorization

Data augmentation — generating synthetic training samples for rare classes

Super-resolution — SRGAN upscales images with perceptual quality

Domain adaptation — transferring appearance between domains (sim-to-real)

Inpainting / editing — filling missing regions or modifying attributes

When to Avoid GANs

Density estimation needed — GANs do not model explicit probabilities (use VAEs or normalizing flows)

Small datasets — GANs are extremely data-hungry; D will memorize with few samples

Exact reconstruction required — GANs are generative, not reconstructive (use autoencoders)

Stable, reproducible training needed — GAN training is inherently unstable and non-deterministic

Text generation — discrete tokens break gradient flow; transformers dominate here

Limited compute — high-resolution GANs require significant GPU memory and time

Strengths

  • Sample quality: Produces the sharpest, most photorealistic samples of any generative model family
  • Implicit density modeling: No restrictive assumptions about data distribution shape
  • Latent space control: Smooth interpolation, disentangled attributes (especially StyleGAN)
  • Versatility: Applicable to images, video, audio, 3D shapes, molecular design
  • No Markov chains: Single forward pass for generation (fast inference)

Limitations

  • Mode collapse: Generator may only learn a subset of the data distribution
  • Training instability: Sensitive to hyperparameters, architecture choices, and learning rate ratios
  • No likelihood estimate: Cannot evaluate how probable a given sample is under the model
  • Evaluation difficulty: FID and IS are imperfect proxies; no single metric captures quality + diversity
  • Vanishing gradients: If D is too strong, G receives uninformative gradients and stops learning
  • Largely superseded for some tasks: Diffusion models now match or exceed GAN quality with more stable training
