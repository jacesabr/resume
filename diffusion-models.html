Diffusion Models

Learning to generate data by reversing a gradual noising process. The mathematical framework behind DALL-E, Stable Diffusion, Imagen, and the modern generative AI revolution.

Mathematical Foundation

Diffusion models are rooted in non-equilibrium thermodynamics. The core idea: define a forward process that gradually destroys data by adding noise, then learn a reverse process that reconstructs data from noise. The mathematics are elegant and surprisingly tractable.

Forward Process (Noise Addition)

The forward process \( q \) adds Gaussian noise to data over \( T \) timesteps. Each step is a simple Gaussian transition:

\( q(\mathbf{x}_t | \mathbf{x}_{t-1}) = \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x}_t; \sqrt{1-\beta_t}\,\mathbf{x}_{t-1},\; \beta_t \mathbf{I}) \)

Here \( \beta_t \in (0, 1) \) is the noise schedule, controlling how much noise is injected at each step. A key property is that we can sample any \( \mathbf{x}_t \) directly from \( \mathbf{x}_0 \) without iterating through all intermediate steps. Define \( \alpha_t = 1 - \beta_t \) and \( \bar{\alpha}_t = \prod_{s=1}^{t} \alpha_s \), then:

\( q(\mathbf{x}_t | \mathbf{x}_0) = \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x}_t; \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}\,\mathbf{x}_0,\; (1-\bar{\alpha}_t)\mathbf{I}) \)

This means: \( \mathbf{x}_t = \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}\,\mathbf{x}_0 + \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}\,\boldsymbol{\epsilon} \), where \( \boldsymbol{\epsilon} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I}) \). As \( t \to T \), the signal vanishes and \( \mathbf{x}_T \approx \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I}) \).

Reverse Process (Learned Denoising)

The reverse process \( p_\theta \) learns to undo each noising step. It is parameterized as a Gaussian whose mean and variance are predicted by a neural network:

\( p_\theta(\mathbf{x}_{t-1} | \mathbf{x}_t) = \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x}_{t-1};\; \boldsymbol{\mu}_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t),\; \sigma_t^2 \mathbf{I}) \)

In practice, the network predicts the noise \( \boldsymbol{\epsilon}_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t) \) rather than the mean directly. The mean is then recovered as:

\( \boldsymbol{\mu}_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\alpha_t}} \left( \mathbf{x}_t - \frac{\beta_t}{\sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}}\,\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t) \right) \)
Training Objective (Simplified Loss)

The full variational lower bound (ELBO) decomposes into per-timestep KL divergence terms. Ho et al. (2020) showed that a simplified objective works remarkably well in practice:

\( L_{\text{simple}} = \mathbb{E}_{t, \mathbf{x}_0, \boldsymbol{\epsilon}} \left[ \| \boldsymbol{\epsilon} - \boldsymbol{\epsilon}_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t) \|^2 \right] \)

The training procedure is beautifully simple: sample a clean image \( \mathbf{x}_0 \), pick a random timestep \( t \), add the corresponding noise to get \( \mathbf{x}_t \), and train the network to predict the noise that was added. That is the entire algorithm.

Noise Schedule

Linear schedule: \( \beta_t \) increases linearly from \( \beta_1 = 10^{-4} \) to \( \beta_T = 0.02 \). Cosine schedule (Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021) produces smoother degradation and better FID scores, especially for low-resolution images.

Variance Choice

The variance \( \sigma_t^2 \) can be fixed to \( \beta_t \) (DDPM default) or to \( \tilde{\beta}_t = \frac{1-\bar{\alpha}_{t-1}}{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}\beta_t \). Learned variance interpolation between these bounds (Nichol & Dhariwal) reduces the number of required sampling steps.

The Diffusion Process

The ink-in-water analogy. Imagine placing a single drop of ink into a glass of still water. At first, the ink has a clear, structured form -- you can see the drop, its tendrils, its shape. Over time, the ink diffuses outward. The structure dissolves. Eventually, the water becomes a uniform, featureless gray. Every trace of the original drop is gone.

This is the forward process. A clean, structured image (the ink drop) is gradually corrupted with Gaussian noise (the diffusion through water) until it becomes pure static -- indistinguishable from random noise. The process is trivial to define and requires no learning.

Now imagine you could reverse time on that glass of water. Watch the uniform gray spontaneously coalesce, tendrils forming, structure emerging, until you recover the exact original ink drop. This reversal -- reconstructing structure from chaos -- is the reverse process, and it is what the neural network learns to do.

The key insight is that each individual reverse step is small. The network does not need to hallucinate an entire image from nothing. It only needs to answer: "Given this slightly noisy version, what does the slightly less noisy version look like?" Repeated 1,000 times, these tiny denoising steps accumulate into the generation of a complete, coherent image.

Forward Process (Destruction)

No parameters to learn. Start with a clean data sample \( \mathbf{x}_0 \). At each of \( T \) timesteps, add a small amount of Gaussian noise according to the noise schedule. By step \( T \) (typically 1000), the result is indistinguishable from pure noise \( \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I}) \). The process is a fixed Markov chain.

Reverse Process (Generation)

Learned via neural network. Start from pure noise \( \mathbf{x}_T \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I}) \). At each step, the model predicts and removes a small amount of noise. After \( T \) steps, the result is a clean, realistic sample \( \mathbf{x}_0 \). Each step is a learned Gaussian transition conditioned on the current noisy input and the timestep.

Why This Works

The forward process has a known, tractable posterior \( q(\mathbf{x}_{t-1}|\mathbf{x}_t, \mathbf{x}_0) \). When each step adds only a tiny amount of noise, the reverse conditional is also approximately Gaussian (Feller, 1949). This means a neural network parameterizing a Gaussian can closely approximate each reverse step. The small step size is what makes the problem tractable -- large jumps would require modeling arbitrarily complex distributions.

Architecture Overview

The noise prediction network \( \boldsymbol{\epsilon}_\theta \) is almost universally a U-Net -- an encoder-decoder architecture with skip connections. The U-Net was originally designed for medical image segmentation, but its ability to preserve spatial detail while capturing global context makes it ideal for denoising.

U-Net Backbone

The encoder downsamples the input through a series of residual blocks and attention layers, compressing the spatial dimensions while expanding channels (e.g., 64 → 128 → 256 → 512). The decoder upsamples symmetrically, and skip connections concatenate encoder features to decoder layers at each resolution, preserving fine-grained spatial information that would otherwise be lost in the bottleneck.

Timestep Conditioning

The network must know which timestep it is denoising at, because the amount and character of noise varies with \( t \). The timestep is embedded using sinusoidal positional encoding (borrowed from Transformers), then projected through a linear layer and added to each residual block via adaptive normalization (AdaGN) or simple addition. This tells each layer how aggressive to be in its denoising.

Self-Attention Layers

Inserted at lower resolutions (16x16 or 32x32) within the U-Net. These allow the model to capture long-range spatial dependencies -- ensuring that, for example, both eyes in a face are consistent even though they are far apart in pixel space. At higher resolutions, convolutions alone handle local patterns efficiently.

The Denoising Loop

At inference time, start with \( \mathbf{x}_T \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I}) \). For each \( t = T, T-1, \ldots, 1 \): feed \( \mathbf{x}_t \) and \( t \) into the U-Net to predict \( \boldsymbol{\epsilon}_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t) \), compute \( \boldsymbol{\mu}_\theta \), sample \( \mathbf{x}_{t-1} \sim \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}_\theta, \sigma_t^2\mathbf{I}) \). After \( T \) steps, \( \mathbf{x}_0 \) is the generated image. This is ancestral sampling.

Latent Diffusion (Stable Diffusion)

Running the full diffusion loop in pixel space for 512x512 images is computationally prohibitive. Latent Diffusion Models (Rombach et al., 2022) first compress images to a low-dimensional latent space using a pre-trained VAE encoder (e.g., 512x512x3 → 64x64x4), run the entire diffusion process in this compact space, then decode back to pixels. This reduces computation by roughly 50x while maintaining image quality.

Key Concepts

Forward Process

The fixed Markov chain that gradually adds noise to data over \( T \) timesteps. Each step is a small Gaussian perturbation. The entire chain has a closed-form solution, allowing direct sampling of any \( \mathbf{x}_t \) from \( \mathbf{x}_0 \) without iteration. This is critical for efficient training.

Reverse Process

The learned Markov chain that iteratively denoises from \( \mathbf{x}_T \) back to \( \mathbf{x}_0 \). Each transition is a learned Gaussian whose parameters are output by a neural network. The reverse process is what we sample from at generation time.

Noise Schedule

The sequence \( \beta_1, \ldots, \beta_T \) controlling how much noise is added at each step. Linear: simple ramp from small to large. Cosine: smoother curve that preserves more signal at early steps, yielding better sample quality. The schedule directly determines \( \bar{\alpha}_t \) and thus the signal-to-noise ratio at each timestep.

U-Net Architecture

Encoder-decoder with skip connections at each resolution level. The encoder captures semantics, the decoder reconstructs spatial detail, and the skips preserve fine structure. ResNet blocks + self-attention + timestep conditioning form the standard recipe. Modern variants add cross-attention for text conditioning.

Classifier-Free Guidance (CFG)

A technique to improve sample quality and text alignment without a separate classifier. During training, the text condition is randomly dropped (replaced with null) some fraction of the time. At inference, two predictions are made -- conditional and unconditional -- and the output is extrapolated: \( \hat{\boldsymbol{\epsilon}} = \boldsymbol{\epsilon}_\text{uncond} + w \cdot (\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_\text{cond} - \boldsymbol{\epsilon}_\text{uncond}) \). The guidance scale \( w \) (typically 7-15) controls the quality/diversity tradeoff.

DDPM vs DDIM

DDPM (Ho et al., 2020): stochastic sampling with \( T = 1000 \) steps. High quality but slow. DDIM (Song et al., 2021): a deterministic, non-Markovian generalization that uses the same trained model but skips steps. DDIM can generate in 50-100 steps with minimal quality loss, and its determinism enables meaningful latent space interpolation.

Latent Diffusion

Instead of operating on raw pixels, diffuse in the latent space of a pre-trained VAE. A 512x512 image becomes a 64x64 latent, reducing the computational cost by orders of magnitude. The VAE is trained separately and frozen during diffusion training. This is the architecture behind Stable Diffusion and most modern text-to-image systems.

Text Conditioning via CLIP

Text prompts are encoded using a frozen CLIP text encoder (or T5) into a sequence of token embeddings. These embeddings are injected into the U-Net via cross-attention layers: the image features attend to the text tokens, allowing the model to steer generation toward the described content. CLIP was pre-trained on 400M image-text pairs, providing rich semantic representations.

Code Example 1 — Simple DDPM Training on MNIST

A minimal but complete DDPM implementation in PyTorch. We define a noise schedule, a small U-Net noise predictor, and the training loop that learns to predict the noise added to MNIST digits. This is the core algorithm from Ho et al. (2020) distilled to its essentials.

Python — ddpm_mnist.py 
import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.nn.functional as F
from torch.utils.data import DataLoader
from torchvision import datasets, transforms
import math

# ─── Noise Schedule ───────────────────────────────────────────
T = 1000
beta_start = 1e-4
beta_end   = 0.02

betas = torch.linspace(beta_start, beta_end, T)          # linear schedule
alphas = 1.0 - betas
alpha_bar = torch.cumprod(alphas, dim=0)                  # cumulative product
sqrt_alpha_bar     = torch.sqrt(alpha_bar)
sqrt_one_minus_ab  = torch.sqrt(1.0 - alpha_bar)

# ─── Sinusoidal Timestep Embedding ────────────────────────────
class SinusoidalPE(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, dim):
        super().__init__()
        self.dim = dim

    def forward(self, t):
        device = t.device
        half = self.dim // 2
        emb = math.log(10000) / (half - 1)
        emb = torch.exp(torch.arange(half, device=device) * -emb)
        emb = t[:, None].float() * emb[None, :]
        return torch.cat([torch.sin(emb), torch.cos(emb)], dim=-1)

# ─── Simplified U-Net for 28x28 Grayscale ─────────────────────
class SimpleUNet(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, ch=64, time_dim=256):
        super().__init__()
        self.time_mlp = nn.Sequential(
            SinusoidalPE(time_dim),
            nn.Linear(time_dim, time_dim),
            nn.GELU(),
        )
        # Encoder
        self.enc1 = nn.Sequential(nn.Conv2d(1, ch, 3, padding=1), nn.GroupNorm(8, ch), nn.GELU())
        self.enc2 = nn.Sequential(nn.Conv2d(ch, ch*2, 3, stride=2, padding=1), nn.GroupNorm(8, ch*2), nn.GELU())
        self.enc3 = nn.Sequential(nn.Conv2d(ch*2, ch*4, 3, stride=2, padding=1), nn.GroupNorm(8, ch*4), nn.GELU())
        # Time projection
        self.t_proj = nn.Linear(time_dim, ch*4)
        # Decoder
        self.dec3 = nn.Sequential(nn.ConvTranspose2d(ch*4, ch*2, 4, stride=2, padding=1), nn.GroupNorm(8, ch*2), nn.GELU())
        self.dec2 = nn.Sequential(nn.ConvTranspose2d(ch*4, ch, 4, stride=2, padding=1), nn.GroupNorm(8, ch), nn.GELU())
        self.dec1 = nn.Conv2d(ch*2, 1, 1)  # output noise prediction

    def forward(self, x, t):
        t_emb = self.time_mlp(t)
        # Encoder path
        e1 = self.enc1(x)          # [B, 64, 28, 28]
        e2 = self.enc2(e1)         # [B, 128, 14, 14]
        e3 = self.enc3(e2)         # [B, 256, 7, 7]
        # Add timestep embedding at bottleneck
        e3 = e3 + self.t_proj(t_emb)[:, :, None, None]
        # Decoder path with skip connections
        d3 = self.dec3(e3)                          # [B, 128, 14, 14]
        d2 = self.dec2(torch.cat([d3, e2], dim=1))  # [B, 64, 28, 28]
        d1 = self.dec1(torch.cat([d2, e1], dim=1))  # [B, 1, 28, 28]
        return d1

# ─── Forward Diffusion: Add Noise ─────────────────────────────
def q_sample(x_0, t, noise=None):
    """Sample x_t from q(x_t | x_0) using the reparameterization trick."""
    if noise is None:
        noise = torch.randn_like(x_0)
    s_ab = sqrt_alpha_bar[t][:, None, None, None].to(x_0.device)
    s_om = sqrt_one_minus_ab[t][:, None, None, None].to(x_0.device)
    return s_ab * x_0 + s_om * noise

# ─── Training Loop ────────────────────────────────────────────
device = "cuda" if torch.cuda.is_available() else "cpu"
model = SimpleUNet().to(device)
optimizer = torch.optim.AdamW(model.parameters(), lr=2e-4)

transform = transforms.Compose([
    transforms.ToTensor(),
    transforms.Normalize((0.5,), (0.5,))  # scale to [-1, 1]
])
dataset = datasets.MNIST("./data", train=True, download=True, transform=transform)
loader  = DataLoader(dataset, batch_size=128, shuffle=True)

for epoch in range(50):
    total_loss = 0.0
    for x_0, _ in loader:
        x_0 = x_0.to(device)
        t = torch.randint(0, T, (x_0.shape[0],), device=device)
        noise = torch.randn_like(x_0)
        x_t = q_sample(x_0, t, noise)

        # Predict the noise that was added
        noise_pred = model(x_t, t)
        loss = F.mse_loss(noise_pred, noise)

        optimizer.zero_grad()
        loss.backward()
        optimizer.step()
        total_loss += loss.item()

    print(f"Epoch {epoch+1} | Loss: {total_loss / len(loader):.4f}")

Training Summary

  • Sample a clean image \( \mathbf{x}_0 \) and a random timestep \( t \sim \text{Uniform}(1, T) \)
  • Noise the image: \( \mathbf{x}_t = \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}\,\mathbf{x}_0 + \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}\,\boldsymbol{\epsilon} \)
  • Predict the noise: \( \hat{\boldsymbol{\epsilon}} = \boldsymbol{\epsilon}_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t) \)
  • Minimize the MSE: \( \| \boldsymbol{\epsilon} - \hat{\boldsymbol{\epsilon}} \|^2 \)
  • That is the entire algorithm. No adversarial training, no mode collapse, no training instability.

Code Example 2 — Sampling with HuggingFace Diffusers

Using the HuggingFace diffusers library to generate images with Stable Diffusion. This demonstrates the high-level API for text-to-image generation, image-to-image refinement, and the effect of classifier-free guidance scale.

Python — stable_diffusion_sampling.py 
import torch
from diffusers import StableDiffusionPipeline, DDIMScheduler
from PIL import Image

# ─── Load Stable Diffusion v2.1 ───────────────────────────────
model_id = "stabilityai/stable-diffusion-2-1"
scheduler = DDIMScheduler.from_pretrained(model_id, subfolder="scheduler")

pipe = StableDiffusionPipeline.from_pretrained(
    model_id,
    scheduler=scheduler,
    torch_dtype=torch.float16,
).to("cuda")

# Enable memory-efficient attention (xformers or sliced)
pipe.enable_attention_slicing()

# ─── Basic Text-to-Image Generation ──────────────────────────
prompt = "A serene Japanese garden at sunset, koi pond reflecting " \
         "cherry blossoms, watercolor style, highly detailed"

image = pipe(
    prompt=prompt,
    num_inference_steps=50,       # DDIM allows fewer steps
    guidance_scale=7.5,            # classifier-free guidance weight
    height=768,
    width=768,
    generator=torch.Generator(device="cuda").manual_seed(42),
).images[0]

image.save("garden_sunset.png")

# ─── Exploring Guidance Scale ─────────────────────────────────
# Low guidance (1-3): more diverse but less prompt-faithful
# Medium guidance (7-8): good balance (default)
# High guidance (15-20): very prompt-faithful but can oversaturate

guidance_scales = [1.0, 3.0, 7.5, 15.0, 20.0]
images = []

for scale in guidance_scales:
    img = pipe(
        prompt="A photorealistic owl wearing a tiny top hat",
        guidance_scale=scale,
        num_inference_steps=30,
        generator=torch.Generator(device="cuda").manual_seed(42),
    ).images[0]
    images.append(img)

# ─── Negative Prompts for Quality Control ─────────────────────
result = pipe(
    prompt="Portrait of a Renaissance queen, oil painting, masterpiece",
    negative_prompt="blurry, low quality, deformed hands, extra fingers, "
                    "watermark, text, cropped, out of frame",
    num_inference_steps=50,
    guidance_scale=9.0,
).images[0]

result.save("renaissance_queen.png")

# ─── Batch Generation with Different Seeds ────────────────────
def generate_variations(prompt, n=4, steps=40):
    """Generate n variations of the same prompt with different seeds."""
    results = []
    for seed in range(n):
        img = pipe(
            prompt=prompt,
            num_inference_steps=steps,
            guidance_scale=7.5,
            generator=torch.Generator(device="cuda").manual_seed(seed),
        ).images[0]
        results.append(img)
    return results

variations = generate_variations(
    "An astronaut riding a horse on Mars, digital art"
)
print(f"Generated {len(variations)} variations")

Code Example 3 — Noise Schedule Visualization

Visualizing the forward diffusion process and comparing linear vs. cosine noise schedules. This code shows how an image is progressively destroyed at different timesteps, and plots the signal-to-noise ratio over time for both schedule types.

Python — noise_schedule_viz.py 
import torch
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from torchvision import datasets, transforms
from torchvision.utils import make_grid

T = 1000

# ─── Define Two Noise Schedules ───────────────────────────────
def linear_schedule(T, beta_start=1e-4, beta_end=0.02):
    """Linear noise schedule from Ho et al. (2020)"""
    betas = torch.linspace(beta_start, beta_end, T)
    alphas = 1.0 - betas
    alpha_bar = torch.cumprod(alphas, dim=0)
    return betas, alpha_bar

def cosine_schedule(T, s=0.008):
    """Cosine schedule from Nichol & Dhariwal (2021)
    Produces smoother noise injection and better results at low resolution."""
    steps = torch.arange(T + 1, dtype=torch.float64)
    f = torch.cos(((steps / T) + s) / (1 + s) * (np.pi / 2)) ** 2
    alpha_bar = f / f[0]
    betas = 1 - (alpha_bar[1:] / alpha_bar[:-1])
    betas = torch.clamp(betas, min=1e-5, max=0.999)
    return betas, alpha_bar[:-1]

betas_lin, ab_lin = linear_schedule(T)
betas_cos, ab_cos = cosine_schedule(T)

# ─── Plot 1: Alpha-Bar Over Time ──────────────────────────────
fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 3, figsize=(18, 5))

axes[0].plot(range(T), ab_lin.numpy(), label="Linear", color="#C15F3C", linewidth=2)
axes[0].plot(range(T), ab_cos.numpy(), label="Cosine", color="#3A7D44", linewidth=2)
axes[0].set_xlabel("Timestep t")
axes[0].set_ylabel("ᾱ(t) — signal remaining")
axes[0].set_title("Cumulative Signal Retention")
axes[0].legend()
axes[0].grid(True, alpha=0.3)

# ─── Plot 2: SNR in Decibels ──────────────────────────────────
snr_lin = ab_lin / (1 - ab_lin)
snr_cos = ab_cos / (1 - ab_cos)

axes[1].plot(range(T), 10 * torch.log10(snr_lin).numpy(),
         label="Linear", color="#C15F3C", linewidth=2)
axes[1].plot(range(T), 10 * torch.log10(snr_cos).numpy(),
         label="Cosine", color="#3A7D44", linewidth=2)
axes[1].set_xlabel("Timestep t")
axes[1].set_ylabel("SNR (dB)")
axes[1].set_title("Signal-to-Noise Ratio")
axes[1].legend()
axes[1].grid(True, alpha=0.3)

# ─── Plot 3: Beta Schedule Comparison ─────────────────────────
axes[2].plot(range(T), betas_lin.numpy(), label="Linear β", color="#C15F3C", linewidth=2)
axes[2].plot(range(T), betas_cos.numpy(), label="Cosine β", color="#3A7D44", linewidth=2)
axes[2].set_xlabel("Timestep t")
axes[2].set_ylabel("β(t)")
axes[2].set_title("Per-Step Noise Magnitude")
axes[2].legend()
axes[2].grid(True, alpha=0.3)

plt.tight_layout()
plt.savefig("noise_schedules.png", dpi=150, bbox_inches="tight")
plt.show()

# ─── Visualize Forward Process on a Real Image ───────────────
dataset = datasets.MNIST("./data", train=True, download=True,
                         transform=transforms.ToTensor())
x_0 = dataset[7][0].unsqueeze(0)  # single digit
x_0 = x_0 * 2 - 1                  # scale to [-1, 1]

timesteps = [0, 50, 100, 250, 500, 750, 999]
noisy_images = []

for t in timesteps:
    noise = torch.randn_like(x_0)
    t_tensor = torch.tensor([t])
    sqrt_ab  = torch.sqrt(ab_lin[t])
    sqrt_oab = torch.sqrt(1 - ab_lin[t])
    x_t = sqrt_ab * x_0 + sqrt_oab * noise
    noisy_images.append((x_t.squeeze() + 1) / 2)  # back to [0,1]

fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, len(timesteps), figsize=(16, 3))
for i, (t, img) in enumerate(zip(timesteps, noisy_images)):
    axes[i].imshow(img.numpy(), cmap="gray", vmin=0, vmax=1)
    axes[i].set_title(f"t = {t}", fontsize=11)
    axes[i].axis("off")

plt.suptitle("Forward Diffusion Process (Linear Schedule)", fontsize=14)
plt.tight_layout()
plt.savefig("forward_process.png", dpi=150, bbox_inches="tight")
plt.show()

What the Visualization Reveals

  • Linear schedule destroys information rapidly in early steps, wasting capacity on nearly-pure-noise timesteps
  • Cosine schedule preserves signal longer, giving the model more useful training signal at intermediate timesteps
  • At \( t = 500 \), the cosine schedule retains roughly twice as much signal as the linear schedule
  • Both converge to pure noise by \( t = T \), but the path there differs significantly

The Diffusion Family

The diffusion framework has spawned a family of models, each building on the core idea of iterative denoising but introducing architectural innovations, efficiency improvements, or new conditioning mechanisms.

DDPM

Ho et al., 2020

The foundational work. Stochastic sampling with 1000 steps. Proved diffusion models can match GANs on image quality. Simple MSE training objective on predicted noise. Set the mathematical framework for everything that followed.

DDIM

Song et al., 2021

Deterministic, non-Markovian sampler compatible with any DDPM-trained model. Enables 10-50x faster sampling by skipping steps. Meaningful latent space interpolation. Same trained model, different inference procedure.

Stable Diffusion

Rombach et al., 2022

Latent diffusion: compress images to 64x64 latents via VAE, diffuse there. CLIP text encoder for conditioning. Open-source weights. Runs on consumer GPUs. The model that democratized AI image generation.

DALL-E 2

Ramesh et al., 2022

Two-stage: CLIP text→image embedding prior + diffusion decoder. Unrolls CLIP image embeddings into full images. Strong text-image alignment via CLIP's contrastive learned space. Introduced inpainting and variations.

Imagen

Saharia et al., 2022

Google's text-to-image model. Uses a frozen T5-XXL text encoder (4.6B params) instead of CLIP. Cascaded diffusion: 64x64→256x256→1024x1024. Showed that scaling the language model matters more than scaling the image model.

ControlNet

Zhang et al., 2023

Adds spatial conditioning to pre-trained diffusion models. Accepts edge maps, depth maps, poses, segmentation masks as input. Copies the encoder of the U-Net and trains only the copy. Zero-convolution initialization preserves the base model's quality.

Flow Matching (Newer Direction)

Lipman et al., 2023 reformulated diffusion as learning a vector field that transports a noise distribution to the data distribution. Rectified flows learn straight-line paths, enabling single-step or few-step generation. Stable Diffusion 3 and Flux use this framework for faster, higher-quality synthesis.

Consistency Models

Song et al., 2023 proposed models that learn to directly map any point on the diffusion trajectory to \( \mathbf{x}_0 \), enabling single-step generation. Can be trained by distillation from a pre-trained diffusion model or from scratch. Trades some quality for dramatic speed improvements.

When to Use / When to Avoid

When to Use Diffusion Models

High-fidelity image generation -- photorealistic or artistic synthesis from text, layouts, or other conditions

Controllable generation -- inpainting, outpainting, style transfer, image editing with ControlNet or IP-Adapter

Mode coverage matters -- diffusion models capture the full data distribution without mode collapse (unlike GANs)

Audio and video synthesis -- WaveGrad for audio, video diffusion for temporal generation

Scientific applications -- molecular design, protein structure generation (e.g., RFdiffusion), material discovery

Super-resolution and restoration -- upscaling, deblurring, denoising where iterative refinement excels

Training stability is critical -- no adversarial dynamics, no discriminator collapse, straightforward MSE loss

When to Avoid Diffusion Models

Real-time generation required -- even 20-step DDIM is slower than a single GAN forward pass; latency-critical applications may need GANs or VAEs

Low compute budget -- training large diffusion models requires significant GPU resources (hundreds of A100-hours for custom models)

Discrete/non-continuous data -- standard diffusion assumes continuous data; text generation is better handled by autoregressive models

Simple distributions -- if a VAE or normalizing flow suffices, diffusion's iterative process adds unnecessary complexity

Exact likelihood needed -- diffusion provides a lower bound (ELBO), not exact log-likelihood; normalizing flows give exact values

Memory-constrained deployment -- the U-Net model is large (~3.4GB for SD v2.1); edge devices may struggle

High-resolution without latent compression -- pixel-space diffusion at 1024x1024+ is prohibitively expensive

The Broader Perspective

Diffusion models have become the dominant paradigm for high-quality image generation, displacing GANs in most applications. Their advantages -- training stability, mode coverage, and controllability -- outweigh their main weakness (slow sampling) in most practical settings. With continued progress in distillation (consistency models), flow matching (rectified flows), and architectural innovations (DiT -- Diffusion Transformers), the sampling speed gap is rapidly closing. Understanding the mathematical foundation covered here provides the basis for working with any member of the diffusion family.

Common Pitfalls

  • Ignoring the noise schedule -- the choice between linear and cosine schedules significantly affects quality; cosine is generally preferred for images below 256x256
  • Too few sampling steps -- reducing below 20 steps without using DDIM/DPM-Solver causes noticeable quality degradation
  • Guidance scale too high -- CFG values above 15-20 produce oversaturated, artifact-heavy images; 7-9 is usually optimal
  • Training on small datasets without augmentation -- diffusion models can memorize training data; regularization and augmentation are essential for small datasets
  • Neglecting the VAE -- in latent diffusion, the VAE quality sets a ceiling; a poor autoencoder means blurry final images regardless of the diffusion model quality
