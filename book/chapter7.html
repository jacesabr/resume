7.1

From Pretraining to Instruction Following

A pretrained language model is, at its core, a completion engine. Feed it the prefix "The capital of France is" and it predicts "Paris" with high confidence. Feed it "What is the capital of France?" and it will often respond with "What is the capital of Germany?" — because that is what question-and-answer pages on the internet look like: questions followed by more questions, exam sheets listing problem after problem, forum threads stacking queries. The model has no concept of "I am the assistant, you are the user." That role structure, that turn-taking convention, that helpful disposition — none of it exists in the pretrained weights. It has to be taught, and the process of teaching it is called instruction tuning or supervised fine-tuning (SFT).

The landmark demonstration came from Wei et al. in 2021 with FLAN (Finetuned Language Models Are Zero-Shot Learners, arXiv 2109.01652). They took a 137B parameter T5 model and fine-tuned it on a mixture of 60 NLP tasks, each described in natural language. The result was striking: on 19 of 25 held-out tasks that the model had never seen during fine-tuning, FLAN outperformed GPT-3 zero-shot despite being evaluated in zero-shot mode itself. The insight was that instruction tuning generalizes — it teaches the model the format of responding to instructions, and that format transfers across tasks. The model was not learning new facts; it was learning a new behavioral mode. The next major step was InstructGPT (Ouyang et al., 2022, arXiv 2203.02155), which combined SFT with human preference learning through Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF). The SFT stage used roughly 13,000 human-written instruction-response pairs; the RLHF stage used human comparisons between model outputs. The remarkable finding was that InstructGPT at 1.3B parameters was preferred by human raters over GPT-3 at 175B — a 100× parameter advantage erased by alignment training. This established that model quality is not solely a function of scale.

The open-source inflection point came with Stanford's Alpaca (Taori et al., 2023). The team took Meta's LLaMA-7B, generated 52,000 instruction-following examples by prompting GPT-3 (the "self-instruct" technique), and fine-tuned the model for roughly $600 in compute. The resulting model matched GPT-3 on a broad range of instruction-following tasks, demonstrating that with the right data, even a fraction of the scale could produce competitive results. This spawned an entire family of open instruction-tuned models: Vicuna, WizardLM, OpenHermes, and eventually Llama-3-Instruct, which used a carefully curated dataset of millions of instruction pairs. One important nuance throughout this history is the "alignment tax": SFT typically reduces raw perplexity by 1–2% compared to the base model (a slight degradation in pure language modeling quality), but it dramatically increases helpfulness, instruction adherence, and safety. The tradeoff is worth it for almost every practical application.

What makes SFT work is that the pretrained model already knows how to write coherent prose, reason about facts, and handle edge cases. SFT does not teach it new knowledge — it teaches it a new mode of operation. By showing the model thousands of examples where a system prompt, a user message, and a well-formed assistant response co-occur in specific token positions, the model learns to condition its generation on the instruction-following frame. The knowledge is already there; SFT is the behavioral key that unlocks it in the right direction.

Key insight: Pretraining gives the model everything it knows. SFT gives it a job description. A 1.3B InstructGPT outperforming a 175B base GPT-3 in human preference ratings is the starkest proof we have that alignment training is not a fine-grained polish — it is a fundamental behavioral shift.

7.2

SFT Dataset Formats

Before you can fine-tune a model, you need data in the right shape. The field has converged on four dominant formats, each reflecting a different philosophy about what the training signal should look like and which model families it targets. Choosing the wrong format is a silent error: the model will train without complaint but will produce malformed outputs at inference time, fail to honor the system prompt, or alternate between two different role-delimiters unpredictably. Understanding the formats — and when to use each — is not academic overhead. It is the first practical decision in every fine-tuning project.

The Alpaca format (Taori et al., 2023) is the simplest. A JSON object with three fields: instruction, input, and output. The input is optional context (a passage to summarize, code to fix) that may be empty for pure instruction-following tasks. The 52,000 Alpaca examples were generated using the "self-instruct" procedure: seed tasks were fed to GPT-3 with a meta-prompt asking it to produce new instruction-input-output triples. This bootstrapping approach let the Stanford team create a large dataset cheaply. The limitation is inherent: Alpaca is single-turn. There is no conversation history, no multi-step reasoning, no system prompt. It excels for task-specific fine-tunes (classification, summarization, rewriting) where a single exchange is sufficient.

The ShareGPT format captures multi-turn conversations as an alternating list. Each example has a conversations key containing a list of objects with from ("human" or "gpt") and value (the text) fields. The dataset was scraped from real ChatGPT conversations shared by users, making it rich in naturalistic multi-turn patterns. The Open Assistant dataset (Köpf et al., 2023, arXiv 2304.07327) follows the same structure with even higher quality: 161,000 messages across 13,500 conversations in 35 languages, all human-written and human-ranked rather than model-generated. A full conversation tree is preserved, enabling sampling of diverse preferred and dispreferred paths. The OpenHermes-2.5 corpus aggregates roughly one million instruction pairs from various public sources (ShareGPT, SlimOrca, Camel-AI, GPT4-Tools) and at the time of its release was the single best source for general-purpose SFT, powering the OpenHermes-2.5-Mistral-7B model that outperformed many larger models on benchmarks.

The ChatML format, standardized by OpenAI and adopted by Mistral, Qwen, and most modern open-source models, uses vocabulary-level special tokens rather than string prefixes. This matters because string-matching approaches fail when content contains text that looks like a role marker. ChatML tokens are actual entries in the vocabulary: <|im_start|> and <|im_end|> are single token IDs, not sequences of characters. Llama-3 uses a different but structurally identical convention with <|begin_of_text|>, <|start_header_id|>, <|end_header_id|>, and <|eot_id|> tokens. HuggingFace's tokenizer.apply_chat_template() handles format serialization automatically via a Jinja2 template bundled with each model's tokenizer config. The data quality insight from Phi-1 (Li et al., 2023, arXiv 2306.11644) is decisive here: a 1.3B model trained on just 6B tokens of "textbook quality" code — carefully filtered and synthetically generated — outperformed much larger models on HumanEval and MBPP coding benchmarks. Quality of training text matters more than raw dataset volume at any given compute budget.

Pick one format, apply it uniformly across all examples, and verify with a random sample before training begins.

7.3

Chat Templates and Masked Loss

Fine-tuning a model on instruction data requires two things that are easy to get wrong: a consistent way to serialize a conversation into a flat token sequence (the chat template), and a training objective that only penalizes the model for mistakes on the tokens it is actually supposed to generate (masked loss). Getting either wrong produces a model that either ignores instructions or degrades into incoherent output after a few turns. Both mistakes are silent: the training loss curves look healthy while the model learns the wrong thing.

The chat template converts a structured conversation — a list of role/content pairs — into a single flat string of tokens. In ChatML, a three-turn conversation becomes: <|im_start|>system\n{system}\n<|im_end|>\n<|im_start|>user\n{user}\n<|im_end|>\n<|im_start|>assistant\n{response}\n<|im_end|>. The model generates tokens starting after the final assistant\n marker. Packing is an important throughput optimization: rather than padding each conversation to the maximum sequence length individually, multiple short conversations are concatenated into a single 2048-token sequence, separated by end-of-conversation tokens. This fills the GPU batch more densely and can improve training speed by 2–3×. The catch is that attention must not leak across conversation boundaries — Flash Attention 2 supports this via document-level attention masks using a cu_seqlens argument that marks where each packed document begins.

Masked loss is the mechanism that makes SFT train the right behavior. Standard cross-entropy loss computes a gradient at every token position. If you apply it naively to a packed conversation sequence, the model receives gradients for predicting user messages given prior user messages — which teaches it to complete instructions rather than respond to them. The fix is to set the label for every non-assistant token to -100. PyTorch's nn.CrossEntropyLoss ignores any position where the target is -100, contributing zero gradient. The masking achieves a 30–50% reduction in effective training tokens for typical instruction datasets (which have roughly equal instruction and response lengths), which also reduces hallucination on template tokens: the model never receives any gradient signal asking it to memorize the exact wording of instructions. TRL's SFTTrainer applies this masking automatically when train_on_inputs=False is set.

masked_loss.py 
from transformers import AutoTokenizer

# ---- Step 1: serialize conversation to flat token string ----
tokenizer = AutoTokenizer.from_pretrained("mistralai/Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2")

messages = [
    {"role": "system",    "content": "You are a helpful assistant."},
    {"role": "user",      "content": "What is backpropagation?"},
    {"role": "assistant", "content": "Backpropagation is the chain-rule algorithm..."},
]

# apply_chat_template inserts special tokens per the model's Jinja2 template
flat_str = tokenizer.apply_chat_template(
    messages,
    tokenize=False,              # ↑ return text string, not IDs — easy to inspect
    add_generation_prompt=False  # ↑ don't add trailing assistant marker
)

input_ids = tokenizer.encode(flat_str, add_special_tokens=False)

# ---- Step 2: build loss mask — -100 everywhere except assistant turn ----
IGNORE = -100  # ↑ PyTorch CrossEntropyLoss silently skips this sentinel value

asst_start_ids = tokenizer.encode(
    "<|im_start|>assistant\n", add_special_tokens=False
)
im_end_id = tokenizer.convert_tokens_to_ids("<|im_end|>")

labels = [IGNORE] * len(input_ids)  # ↑ start fully masked; unmask only assistant tokens
i, in_asst = 0, False

while i < len(input_ids):
    window = input_ids[i : i + len(asst_start_ids)]
    if window == asst_start_ids:          # ↑ entering assistant span — flip flag
        in_asst = True
        i += len(asst_start_ids)
        continue
    if in_asst:
        if input_ids[i] == im_end_id:       # ↑ end of assistant turn — stop computing loss
            in_asst = False
        else:
            labels[i] = input_ids[i]         # ↑ only these positions contribute gradients
    i += 1

active = sum(1 for l in labels if l != IGNORE)
print(f"Total tokens: {len(input_ids)}, Active for loss: {active} ({100*active/len(input_ids):.1f}%)")
The masking rule in one sentence: label[i] = input_ids[i] if token i is part of an assistant turn, else label[i] = -100. This single change converts a general next-token predictor into an instruction follower.
▶ Try it — implement masked cross-entropy on a toy token sequence 
import math

IGNORE = -100

def softmax(logits):
    m = max(logits)
    exps = [math.exp(x - m) for x in logits]
    s = sum(exps)
    return [e / s for e in exps]

def cross_entropy(logits, target):
    probs = softmax(logits)
    return -math.log(max(probs[target], 1e-9))

# Toy: 7 positions, vocab=5
# Positions 0-2 = system+user (MASKED), positions 3-6 = assistant (TRAINED)
logits = [
    [2.1, 0.5, 0.3, 0.1, 0.0],  # pos 0: system   (masked)
    [0.1, 3.2, 0.2, 0.1, 0.1],  # pos 1: user t1  (masked)
    [0.4, 0.3, 2.1, 0.1, 0.1],  # pos 2: user t2  (masked)
    [0.2, 0.1, 4.1, 0.3, 0.2],  # pos 3: asst t1  (trained) target=2
    [0.1, 0.2, 0.1, 3.8, 0.1],  # pos 4: asst t2  (trained) target=3
    [0.3, 0.1, 0.2, 0.1, 2.9],  # pos 5: asst t3  (trained) target=4
    [1.1, 0.2, 3.1, 0.1, 0.1],  # pos 6: EOS      (trained) target=2
]
targets = [IGNORE, IGNORE, IGNORE, 2, 3, 4, 2]

print(f"{'Pos':<5} {'Role':<12} {'Target':<8} {'Loss':<10} {'Notes'}")
print("-" * 55)

total, n_active = 0.0, 0
for i, (lg, tgt) in enumerate(zip(logits, targets)):
    role = "system/user" if i < 3 else ("EOS" if i == 6 else "assistant")
    if tgt == IGNORE:
        print(f"{i:<5} {role:<12} {'MASKED':<8} {'---':<10} no gradient")
    else:
        loss = cross_entropy(lg, tgt)
        total += loss; n_active += 1
        prob = softmax(lg)[tgt]
        print(f"{i:<5} {role:<12} {tgt:<8} {loss:<10.4f} p(correct)={prob:.3f}")

print()
print(f"Average loss over {n_active} active positions: {total/n_active:.4f}")
print(f"Effective token reduction from masking:        {100*(len(targets)-n_active)/len(targets):.0f}%")
print()

# Compare: if we naively trained on ALL positions
full_loss = sum(cross_entropy(lg, tgt if tgt != IGNORE else 0)
               for lg, tgt in zip(logits, targets)) / len(targets)
print(f"Naive unmasked loss (avg over all {len(targets)} pos): {full_loss:.4f}")
print("The unmasked loss includes gradients from user tokens —")
print("training the model to predict what users say, not what assistants say.")

7.4

LoRA in Depth

Full fine-tuning of a large language model is prohibitively expensive. A 7B-parameter model in 16-bit precision requires 14 GB just to store the weights. The Adam optimizer maintains two first- and second-moment estimates per parameter, adding another 28 GB. Activation checkpointing helps but backpropagation still requires forward-pass activations during the backward pass. In practice, full fine-tuning a 7B model demands 80+ GB of GPU memory. Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA), introduced by Hu et al. in 2021 (arXiv 2106.09685), resolves this through a mathematically elegant decomposition: rather than updating the full weight matrix W, learn a low-rank decomposition of the update delta.

The key empirical observation is that when you fine-tune a pretrained model on a downstream task, the resulting weight changes have a low intrinsic rank — they span a small subspace of the full parameter space. LoRA exploits this by parameterizing the update as ΔW = B · A, where B ∈ ℝd×r and A ∈ ℝr×k, and r << min(d, k) is the rank. The forward pass becomes h = W0x + (α/r) · B · A · x, where W0 is frozen and only B and A are updated. At initialization, A is drawn from a Gaussian distribution N(0, σ2) and B is set to zero, so the adapter contributes exactly zero at the start of training — the model begins with the same behavior as the pretrained baseline, which is critical for stability.

The parameter savings are substantial. For the query projection in LLaMA-2 7B attention: d = k = 4096, so the full W has 4096 × 4096 = 16,777,216 parameters. With LoRA at r=16, A has 4096 × 16 = 65,536 parameters and B has 16 × 4096 = 65,536 parameters, for a total of 131,072 — 128× fewer than the full matrix. Across all 32 layers with Q and V projections adapted (the standard starting point), a 7B model LoRA fine-tune at r=16 introduces roughly 4.2 million trainable parameters out of 7 billion: 0.06% of the model. This reduces GPU memory from 80+ GB to approximately 5 GB, enabling fine-tuning on a single consumer GPU. The scaling factor α/r (where α is typically set to twice the rank) controls how strongly the adapter contribution is weighted; setting α=r is equivalent to no scaling, while α=2r doubles the adapter contribution relative to the frozen weights.

Which weight matrices to adapt is an empirical question that Hu et al. addressed through ablations. The standard practice is to apply LoRA to the query (Wq) and value (Wv) projection matrices in every attention layer. Adding the key matrix (Wk) and output projection (Wo) gives marginal gains for most tasks at the cost of more parameters. Adapting the feed-forward network (FFN) up/down projections helps on knowledge-intensive tasks but is less efficient per parameter. Embedding layers should be left frozen: they are large (vocab_size × d_model), sparse in gradients, and slow to converge. After training, the adapter can be merged into the base weights with zero inference overhead: Wmerged = W0 + (α/r) · B · A. The merged model is architecturally identical to the original and can be served by any standard runtime without adapter-aware infrastructure.

lora_from_scratch.py 
import random, math

class LoRALayer:
    """LoRA adapter for one linear layer. Pure Python — no PyTorch needed."""

    def __init__(self, d_in, d_out, rank=16, alpha=32):
        self.rank  = rank                    # ↑ bottleneck dimension — lower = fewer params
        self.scale = alpha / rank             # ↑ α/r scaling factor; α=2r → scale=2.0

        # A: small random init — contributes to forward pass from step 1
        sigma = 1 / math.sqrt(rank)
        self.A = [[random.gauss(0, sigma) for _ in range(d_out)]
                   for _ in range(rank)]    # ↑ shape: (rank, d_out)

        # B: zero init — adapter output is exactly 0 at training start
        self.B = [[0.0 for _ in range(rank)]
                   for _ in range(d_in)]     # ↑ shape: (d_in, rank)

        self.d_in  = d_in
        self.d_out = d_out

    def trainable_params(self):
        return self.rank * self.d_out + self.d_in * self.rank  # ↑ A params + B params

    def base_params(self):
        return self.d_in * self.d_out          # ↑ W0 is frozen — these never update

    def compression_ratio(self):
        return self.base_params() / self.trainable_params()

# Example: LLaMA-2 7B query projection
layer = LoRALayer(d_in=4096, d_out=4096, rank=16, alpha=32)
print(f"Base W params:      {layer.base_params():>12,}")

QLoRA: Quantized Fine-Tuning

LoRA dramatically reduces the number of trainable parameters, but the frozen base model still needs to fit in GPU memory during training. A 7B model in BF16 requires 14 GB; a 65B model requires 130 GB. QLoRA, introduced by Dettmers et al. in 2023 (arXiv 2305.14314, NeurIPS 2023), resolves this by quantizing the frozen base model to 4 bits while keeping the LoRA adapters in 16-bit precision. The headline result: fine-tune a 65B model on a single 48 GB GPU (an A6000 or A100 PCIe) — a task that previously required a cluster of eight 80 GB A100s. A 7B fine-tune fits on a 10 GB consumer GPU.

The 4-bit format that makes this possible is called NF4 (Normal Float 4-bit). Uniform 4-bit quantization divides the weight range into 16 equally spaced bins. NF4 places its 16 quantization levels non-uniformly — they are positioned at the quantile boundaries of a standard normal distribution N(0,1). This matters because pretrained neural network weights approximately follow a normal distribution: most weights are close to zero, with exponentially fewer weights at larger magnitudes. NF4's denser grid near zero minimizes quantization error where most of the weight mass actually sits, making it the information-theoretically optimal 4-bit data type for normally distributed values. The quantization error of NF4 versus full BF16 is approximately half that of uniform 4-bit (INT4) for typical weight distributions.

QLoRA introduces two additional optimizations on top of NF4. Double quantization quantizes the quantization constants themselves: the per-block scaling factors that NF4 needs are stored in FP32 by default, adding 32 bits per 64 weights (0.5 bits/weight overhead). Double quantization compresses these constants from FP32 to 8-bit, reducing the overhead to approximately 0.127 bits/weight. This saves roughly 0.37 bits per parameter additional compression. Paged optimizers use CUDA's unified memory to spill Adam optimizer states from GPU VRAM to CPU RAM during gradient update steps. Normally, AdamW stores two FP32 copies of gradients (first and second moments), requiring 8 bytes per parameter. For a 7B model, that is 56 GB just for optimizer states. Paged optimizers let the GPU use CPU RAM as an overflow buffer, preventing OOM crashes during the gradient spikes that occur at the start of training. The dequantize-compute-requantize cycle: NF4 weights are decompressed to BF16 on the fly for each forward and backward pass through a layer, then stored back in NF4. This adds ~10–15% wall-clock overhead versus full BF16 training but cuts memory by 4×.

import math

# NF4 places 16 quantization levels at the quantile boundaries
# of a standard normal distribution N(0,1).
# This demo computes those levels and compares quantization error
# against uniform 4-bit quantization for typical weight distributions.

def normal_cdf(x):
    """Approximate CDF of standard normal distribution."""
    t = 1.0 / (1.0 + 0.2316419 * abs(x))
    d = 0.3989422820 * math.exp(-0.5 * x * x)
    p = d * t * (0.3193815 + t * (-0.3565638 + t * (1.7814779 +
        t * (-1.8212560 + t * 1.3302744))))
    return 1.0 - p if x > 0 else p

def normal_ppf(p, tol=1e-6):
    """Inverse normal CDF (quantile function) via bisection."""
    lo, hi = -6.0, 6.0
    for _ in range(60):
        mid = (lo + hi) / 2
        if normal_cdf(mid) < p:
            lo = mid
        else:
            hi = mid
    return (lo + hi) / 2

# NF4: 16 evenly spaced quantile levels under N(0,1)
n_levels = 16
nf4_levels = [normal_ppf((i + 0.5) / n_levels) for i in range(n_levels)]

# Uniform 4-bit: 16 evenly spaced values in [-1, 1]
w_min, w_max = min(nf4_levels), max(nf4_levels)
uniform_levels = [w_min + (w_max - w_min) * i / (n_levels - 1) for i in range(n_levels)]

def quantize(value, levels):
    """Find nearest quantization level."""
    return min(levels, key=lambda l: abs(l - value))

def quant_error(levels, n_samples=10000):
    """Average squared quantization error for N(0,1) samples."""
    # Use deterministic grid samples instead of random
    step = 6.0 / n_samples
    total_err = 0.0
    count = 0
    for i in range(n_samples):
        x = -3.0 + i * step
        # Weight by N(0,1) density
        density = math.exp(-0.5 * x * x) / math.sqrt(2 * math.pi)
        q = quantize(x, levels)
        total_err += density * (x - q) ** 2 * step
        count += 1
    return total_err

unif_err = quant_error(uniform_levels)

Direct Preference Optimization (DPO)

Supervised fine-tuning teaches the model to follow instructions, but it does not teach the model to follow good instructions while refusing bad ones, to prefer detailed responses over terse ones, or to align its style with human expectations about tone and format. For that, you need preference learning — training from human judgments about which of two responses is better. The classical approach is RLHF: train a reward model on human preference pairs, then use PPO to optimize the policy against the reward model while penalizing divergence from the SFT reference. This works but is complex: it requires separate reward model training, an online generation loop, PPO's finicky hyperparameters, and careful monitoring for reward hacking. Direct Preference Optimization (DPO), introduced by Rafailov et al. in 2023 (arXiv 2305.18290, NeurIPS 2023), eliminates the reward model and the RL loop entirely.

The DPO insight is mathematical rather than architectural. The KL-constrained RL objective that RLHF optimizes — maximize expected reward while staying close to the reference policy — has a closed-form optimal solution. The optimal policy is: π*(y|x) ∝ πref(y|x) × exp(r(x,y)/β), where r(x,y) is the true reward and β controls the divergence penalty. By substituting this relationship into the Bradley-Terry preference model (which states that the probability of human raters preferring yw over yl is σ(r(yw) - r(yl))), and re-expressing reward in terms of the optimal policy, the reward model drops out entirely. What remains is a direct supervised loss on preference pairs.

DPO LOSS LDPO = -E[ log σ( β · (log πθ(yw|x)/πref(yw|x) − log πθ(yl|x)/πref(yl|x)) ) ] yw = chosen response, yl = rejected response, πref = SFT reference policy (frozen), β = KL divergence coefficient (typically 0.1).

The loss has a natural interpretation: it increases the log-probability of the chosen response relative to the reference policy, while decreasing the log-probability of the rejected response — all in a single supervised pass. The ratio log πθ(y|x)/πref(y|x) is called the implicit reward: the model is acting as its own reward function. The β hyperparameter controls how far the trained policy is allowed to diverge from the SFT reference. At β=0.1 (the most common setting), the policy has significant freedom to move. Higher β forces the policy to stay closer to the SFT checkpoint, trading alignment flexibility for coherence. The data format for DPO is simple: triples of (prompt, chosen_response, rejected_response). Chosen responses are human-preferred or GPT-4-preferred; rejected responses are the same model's less-preferred output or a lower-quality alternative.

DPO has important practical advantages over RLHF. There is no reward model to train (saving 1–3 days of compute on typical datasets). There is no PPO sampling loop (which requires generating responses online at each training step). The loss is deterministic and stable — standard SGD without the variance amplification of policy gradient methods. TRL's DPOTrainer implements this in roughly 50 lines: it loads the SFT checkpoint as the frozen reference policy, computes log-probabilities for chosen and rejected under both the training model and the reference, and computes the DPO loss. Notable variants include IPO (Azar et al., 2023), which regularizes DPO to prevent over-optimization, and KTO (Ethayarajh et al., 2024), which extends DPO to work with unpaired feedback — thumbs-up or thumbs-down on individual responses rather than pairwise comparisons.

dpo_loss.py 
import math

def sigmoid(x):
    return 1 / (1 + math.exp(-x))

def dpo_loss(log_prob_w_policy, log_prob_w_ref,
              log_prob_l_policy, log_prob_l_ref, beta=0.1):
    """
    Compute the DPO loss for one preference pair.
    All log_probs are sum of log-probabilities over response tokens.
    """
    # Implicit reward = difference in log-prob ratio between chosen and rejected
    reward_w = beta * (log_prob_w_policy - log_prob_w_ref)  # ↑ chosen response "reward"
    reward_l = beta * (log_prob_l_policy - log_prob_l_ref)  # ↑ rejected response "reward"

    # DPO loss = -log sigmoid(reward_w - reward_l)
    # Gradient increases p(chosen) and decreases p(rejected) relative to reference
    margin = reward_w - reward_l                            # ↑ positive = chosen preferred
    loss = -math.log(sigmoid(margin) + 1e-9)            # ↑ loss is low when margin is large

    return loss, reward_w, reward_l, margin

pairs = [

     -14.0, -14.0, -14.0, -14.0),  # ↑ no change from reference — loss = log(2) ≈ 0.693
    ("Bad (policy prefers rejected over chosen)",
     -16.5, -13.8, -13.2, -14.2),  # ↑ policy has inverted preference — high loss
]

RLHF vs DPO

Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) as applied to language models was formalized by Christiano et al. in 2017 (arXiv 1706.03741) and scaled to production by InstructGPT (Ouyang et al., 2022). The pipeline has three stages: (1) SFT on demonstration data; (2) train a reward model (RM) on human preference pairs using the Bradley-Terry model, where P(yw > yl) = σ(r(yw) - r(yl)); (3) optimize the policy using PPO to maximize RM(y) while penalizing KL divergence from the SFT reference. The full objective is: L = E[RM(y)] - β × KL(πθ || πref). InstructGPT used β=0.02 for the PPO KL coefficient. The reward model is itself a language model (GPT-3 175B in InstructGPT's case) fine-tuned to output a scalar score. Training it requires ~33,000 pairwise comparisons; training the PPO policy requires another ~31,000 prompt samples and online generation at each step.

RLHF's principal failure mode is reward hacking: the PPO policy discovers inputs that fool the reward model into giving high scores without actually producing high-quality responses. A classic example is length exploitation — reward models trained on human preferences often conflate longer responses with better responses, so the policy learns to produce verbose padding that maximizes RM score without improving quality. Other failure modes include KL divergence drift (without careful tuning, PPO can push the policy so far from the SFT reference that it loses coherence), mode collapse (the policy collapses to a narrow set of high-reward templates), and general training instability from PPO's high variance gradient estimates. The hyperparameter sensitivity is real: different labs have reported that the β coefficient for KL penalty needs to be tuned within a factor of 2 or results degrade substantially.

DPO is strictly simpler and more practical for most teams. The absence of a reward model training stage eliminates a major source of error (RM overfitting to the preference data) and cuts compute roughly in half. The deterministic supervised loss avoids the high-variance policy gradient updates that make PPO difficult to tune. Empirically, DPO matches RLHF on summarization benchmarks (Stiennon et al., 2020 TL;DR dataset) and falls slightly behind on complex multi-step reasoning tasks, where RLHF's ability to use non-differentiable signals (e.g., answer correctness on math problems) gives it an edge. In practice, DPO is now the default first choice for preference learning in open-source fine-tuning. RLHF at scale — with Constitutional AI feedback (section 7.11) and RLAIF replacing human raters — is still used at the frontier by labs like Anthropic and OpenAI where the engineering investment is justified by the quality ceiling.

DPO length exploitation: DPO inherits the same length bias as RLHF if your preference data has longer responses in the chosen column. Mitigation: normalize log-probabilities by response length (logπ/|y|) before computing the DPO ratio, or curate pairs where chosen and rejected have similar lengths.

Evaluation: MMLU, MT-Bench, Elo

A model is only as good as your ability to measure it. The gap between "the model performs well in demo" and "the model performs well on the task that matters" has grown as models have grown more capable at gaming benchmarks. Evaluation for instruction-tuned models requires multiple orthogonal measurements: automated multiple-choice benchmarks for factual coverage, multi-turn judge evaluations for conversational quality, and human preference ratings for real-world utility. No single metric tells the full story.

MMLU (Massive Multitask Language Understanding, Hendrycks et al., 2020, arXiv 2009.03300) remains the most widely reported benchmark for factual and reasoning capabilities. It spans 57 subjects from elementary mathematics to professional law and medicine, drawn from real exams and standardized tests: 14,042 multiple-choice questions, each with four options. The random-chance baseline is 25%. Key reference scores: GPT-3 (original, 175B) = 43.9%; GPT-3.5-turbo = 70.0%; GPT-4 = 86.4%; LLaMA-2 70B = 68.9%; Mistral-7B = 60.1%. MMLU is a useful capability ceiling probe, but it has two well-documented weaknesses. First, it is heavily contaminated: Zhou et al. (2023) estimated that more than 50% of MMLU test questions appear verbatim or near-verbatim in common training corpora, meaning high scores may reflect memorization. Second, it tests knowledge retrieval more than reasoning — a model can answer many MMLU questions by recognizing the correct option pattern without understanding the underlying concept.

MT-Bench (Zheng et al., 2023, arXiv 2306.05685) addresses conversational quality. It consists of 80 multi-turn questions across 8 categories (writing, roleplay, extraction, reasoning, math, coding, STEM, and humanities), each with two turns — the second turn requires the model to build on its first response. GPT-4 acts as the judge, scoring responses from 1 to 10. Key scores: GPT-4 = 8.99; Claude-v1 = 8.15; GPT-3.5-turbo = 7.94; Vicuna-13B = 6.57; LLaMA-2-13B-Chat = 6.27. MT-Bench is more sensitive to instruction adherence and multi-turn coherence than MMLU, and harder to game through contamination. The Chatbot Arena (Chiang et al., 2024, arXiv 2403.04132) goes further: it compiles over 500,000 anonymous pairwise human comparisons where users choose which of two blind model responses they prefer, and converts these into Elo ratings using the same formula as chess rankings. Elo is more reliable than any automated benchmark because it reflects actual human preference in open-ended conversation, but it is slow to update and expensive to collect.

    """Probability that model A beats model B under Elo model."""
    return 1.0 / (1.0 + 10 ** ((rating_b - rating_a) / 400))

Deploying a Local Assistant

Once you have a fine-tuned model — whether merged weights or a base model plus LoRA adapter — you need a runtime that serves it efficiently. Production deployments at scale use vLLM (Kwon et al., 2023, arXiv 2309.06180) with PagedAttention — a system that manages the KV cache like virtual memory pages, allowing up to 24× higher throughput than naive HuggingFace inference through continuous batching and near-zero KV cache fragmentation. For local deployment, personal projects, and development, two tools dominate: llama.cpp and Ollama.

llama.cpp (Gerganov, 2023) is a pure C++ inference engine that introduced the GGUF format: a self-describing binary file containing quantized weights, tokenizer vocabulary, and model configuration in a single portable file. GGUF replaced the older GGML format (binary incompatible) and is now the standard for local LLM distribution. Quantization tiers: Q2_K (smallest, most degraded), Q4_K_M (recommended sweet spot for most use cases), Q5_K_M (better quality at ~25% larger file), Q8_0 (near-lossless, 2× the 4-bit size). For a LLaMA-2 7B model: Q4_K_M = 4.08 GB, runnable on any 8 GB GPU or recent M1/M2 Mac. The quality degradation from Q4_K_M versus FP16 on MMLU is approximately 0.5–1.0 percentage points — nearly imperceptible for most applications. On an RTX 3090, a 7B Q4 model generates at 40–80 tokens per second; on CPU only, 5–15 tokens per second.

Ollama wraps llama.cpp with a higher-level interface: a model library (with ollama pull commands), a local REST API on port 11434 that is OpenAI-compatible, and automatic model management. You can run any GGUF model with ollama run model_name and point any OpenAI client at http://localhost:11434/v1 without changing client code. For production local deployments requiring multiple concurrent users, vLLM on a cloud GPU (RunPod, Lambda Labs, Vast.ai) outperforms Ollama by an order of magnitude for batched inference — vLLM's PagedAttention enables serving 20–100 concurrent requests on a single A100 while Ollama serves one at a time.

The Complete Fine-Tuning Pipeline

Every technique in this chapter — SFT dataset formats, masked loss, LoRA, QLoRA, DPO, evaluation, deployment — is a piece of a larger pipeline. Understanding each piece in isolation is necessary but not sufficient. The hard part is knowing how they fit together and how to make decisions at each step based on what you actually have: dataset size, available GPU memory, task type, and the quality bar required. This section assembles the pieces into a concrete end-to-end walkthrough, including the tools, hyperparameters, and decision rules that practitioners actually use.

The pipeline begins with base model selection. A strong pretrained model makes everything downstream easier: richer world knowledge, stronger latent instruction-following capability, more signal per training step. For open-source work in 2024–2025, Llama-3-8B, Mistral-7B-v0.3, or Qwen2-7B are strong starting points for the 7B class; Llama-3-70B or Qwen2-72B for the 70B class. The dataset decision tree branches primarily on size and quality. Fewer than 500 high-quality examples: LoRA r=4–8, 3–5 epochs, aggressive early stopping. 500–5,000 examples: LoRA r=16, 3 epochs, then optional DPO with 500–1,000 preference pairs. More than 5,000 examples: QLoRA r=32–64 for memory efficiency or full fine-tune if you have 80+ GB VRAM. Above 50,000 examples: consider full fine-tuning from the base (not instruct) model, as SFT at this scale can overwrite the instruction-following alignment from the RLHF-tuned model. The LIMA finding (Zhou et al., 2023) that 1,000 carefully curated examples match 52,000 Alpaca examples on human preference is not an outlier — it reflects a real phenomenon: past a few thousand quality examples, most gains come from diversity and curation, not from raw count.

The practical toolchain has converged around three libraries. HuggingFace PEFT provides the LoRA and QLoRA implementation: LoraConfig, get_peft_model, prepare_model_for_kbit_training, and merge_and_unload. TRL (Transformer Reinforcement Learning) provides the training loops: SFTTrainer for instruction tuning (handles chat template application, masked loss, packing) and DPOTrainer for preference learning. Axolotl wraps TRL with a YAML-configurable interface that handles multi-GPU sharding, gradient checkpointing, mixed precision, and dataset preprocessing. For a team running repeated fine-tuning experiments, Axolotl is the right abstraction level: a single YAML file fully specifies the experiment, results are reproducible, and the defaults are well-calibrated. The full pipeline from a clean dataset to a deployed GGUF model typically takes 2–6 hours on an A100 40GB, at a compute cost of $3–$15 on cloud GPU providers.

Constitutional AI and RLAIF

Anthropic introduced Constitutional AI (CAI) in 2022 (arXiv 2212.08073) to address a fundamental bottleneck in RLHF: human raters are expensive, slow, inconsistent, and cannot provide feedback on behaviors they have never encountered. CAI replaces human preference annotations with AI-generated feedback, guided by a written "constitution" — a set of principles that the AI should follow. The result is a scalable alignment method that can generate feedback on any model output, at any volume, in any domain, with consistent application of the constitutional principles.

The CAI pipeline has two stages. The first is supervised learning from AI feedback (SL-CAI). The model is first prompted to generate a response to a potentially harmful request. It is then prompted to critique its own response based on a principle drawn from the constitution: "Is this response helpful, harmless, and honest? Does it avoid condescending or paternalistic language? Does it respect the autonomy of the person asking?" The model then revises its response to better satisfy the critique. This critique-revision cycle can be repeated multiple times. The final revised responses are used as supervised training data. The second stage is RLAIF (Reinforcement Learning from AI Feedback): a preference model is trained using AI-generated pairwise comparisons (the model is asked which of two responses better satisfies a constitutional principle), and this preference model is used as a reward signal for PPO training. No human preferences are required for the safety and harmlessness dimensions, though human preferences are still used for helpfulness.

SL-CAI: Critique-Revision Loop

1. Generate initial response (possibly harmful)
2. Critique: "Identify specific ways this is harmful/dishonest"
3. Revise: "Rewrite to avoid identified harms"
4. Repeat critique-revision N times
5. Collect final revisions as SFT training data
6. Fine-tune model on (prompt, final revision) pairs

RLAIF: AI Preference Model

1. Generate response pairs from SL-CAI model
2. Prompt AI: "Which response better satisfies principle X?"
3. Collect AI preference labels at scale
4. Train preference model on AI-labeled pairs
5. PPO using AI preference model as reward
6. Result: "Claude" — helpful, harmless, honest

The practical implications of CAI are significant. First, it scales beyond human feedback: a single researcher can write a 10-principle constitution and generate millions of AI-labeled training pairs automatically. This makes it possible to iterate on alignment objectives quickly — changing a principle rewrites the preference data without re-running human annotation. Second, AI feedback quality scales with model quality: as the base model improves, the quality of its critique and revision improves, creating a virtuous cycle. Anthropic's Claude models are trained with variants of this pipeline. Third, and philosophically important: CAI makes the alignment objectives explicit and auditable. The constitution is a text document that can be inspected, debated, and revised — unlike the implicit preferences of human raters, which are opaque and inconsistent. The limitation is that AI feedback inherits the biases and blind spots of the feedback model. A constitution that fails to anticipate a class of harmful behavior produces a preference model that is blind to that class. This is why human oversight at the constitution-writing stage remains essential.

Practical Tips: What Goes Wrong

Fine-tuning is not a process that either succeeds or fails cleanly. Most of the time it partially succeeds: the model follows instructions in some contexts and ignores them in others, produces excellent outputs on the distribution of your training data and bizarre outputs just outside it, or improves on your target task while degrading on baseline capabilities. Understanding the common failure modes — and how to detect and fix them — is more valuable than any additional technique. This section catalogs what goes wrong in practice.

Mode collapse is the most catastrophic failure mode. The model stops producing diverse outputs and instead produces a narrow set of templates regardless of the input. It can happen during SFT when training examples are too homogeneous (every response starts the same way, has the same length, uses the same hedging phrases), or during DPO when the KL penalty is too weak (β too low) and the policy overshoots. Symptoms: identical or near-identical outputs for varied prompts; perplexity on validation set stops decreasing and starts increasing; evaluation diversity metrics collapse. Detection: prompt with 20 diverse inputs and compute type-token ratio of outputs. Fix: reduce learning rate, increase β for DPO, add more diverse examples, or restart from an earlier checkpoint.

Length exploitation is a specific form of reward hacking that affects both DPO and RLHF. Human raters and AI judges consistently conflate response length with quality, creating a spurious correlation in preference datasets: longer responses tend to be rated as preferred even when the added length is padding. The policy or DPO training procedure learns this and produces verbosely padded responses. This is particularly dangerous in DPO because it manifests as a silent quality regression — the model scores higher on automated benchmarks (MT-Bench judges reward verbosity) while being less useful to actual users. Detection: measure word count ratio between chosen and rejected responses in your preference data; if chosen/rejected > 1.3, you have a length bias. Fix: normalize DPO log-probabilities by response length, or manually balance lengths in your preference dataset. Catastrophic forgetting is the loss of base model capabilities during fine-tuning. A model fine-tuned on customer support data may forget how to write code or solve math problems. This is inherent to the gradient update process: when training examples don't cover a skill, the gradients may point away from the parameters that encode it. LoRA mitigates this substantially (it updates only 0.06% of parameters), but high-rank LoRA or full fine-tuning on narrow datasets can still produce significant forgetting. Detection: eval on MMLU and MT-Bench before and after; track perplexity on held-out general text. Fix: add a small fraction (5–10%) of general-purpose instruction examples to your training set ("replay"), or reduce LoRA rank.

MODE COLLAPSE
Cause: homogeneous training data, or DPO with β too low. Symptom: every output starts the same way. Fix: diverse data, higher β, lower LR.
LENGTH EXPLOITATION
Cause: length bias in preference data (chosen responses longer). Symptom: verbose padding, high benchmark scores but poor real-world quality. Fix: length-normalize DPO loss, balance dataset.
REWARD HACKING (RLHF)
Cause: policy finds inputs that fool the reward model. Symptom: high RM score but low human score; outputs that "look good" but are subtly wrong. Fix: KL penalty, RM ensemble, fresh human evals.
CATASTROPHIC FORGETTING
Cause: narrow training data doesn't cover base capabilities. Symptom: MMLU drops 5%+; model can't do math after customer-service fine-tune. Fix: replay 5-10% general data, use LoRA not full fine-tune.
TEMPLATE HALLUCINATION
Cause: loss computed on instruction tokens (missing mask). Symptom: model generates user-turn text mid-response. Fix: verify masked labels with a print statement before training starts.
FORMAT LEAKAGE
Cause: mixed chat formats in training data (Alpaca + ChatML). Symptom: model outputs "### Response:" or "<|im_end|>" at random. Fix: pick one format, validate every example before training.

What You've Built — and What's Next

Seven chapters ago, a language model was a black box that produced coherent text when you typed into a chat interface. You now have the full picture, from first principles to production deployment. You started with raw text — bytes of web-scraped documents — and you understand how a BPE tokenizer converts them into integer sequences that a neural network can process. You built a transformer from scratch and understand why every component is there: the attention mechanism that lets tokens communicate across arbitrary sequence positions, the positional encodings that inject order information without recurrence, the feed-forward sublayers that perform per-token nonlinear transformation, the layer normalization that stabilizes gradient flow through depth, the residual connections that make very deep networks trainable. You saw how pretraining works at scale: the cross-entropy objective, the Adam optimizer with its adaptive per-parameter learning rates, the cosine learning rate schedule that warms up over hundreds of steps and decays toward zero over millions, the data pipeline that streams shards without ever starving the GPU.

You understand why scale matters — the Chinchilla scaling laws tell you exactly how to allocate compute between model size and data, and why the GPT-3 era training runs were compute-suboptimal. You understand why data quality matters more than data quantity past the first few thousand examples, and why the LIMA paper's 1,000-example result is not a fluke but a consequence of how instruction tuning works: you are reshaping a behavioral mode, not teaching new knowledge. In this chapter, you saw how SFT bridges the gap between completion engine and assistant, how masked loss prevents the model from learning to predict user messages instead of generating responses, how LoRA reduces trainable parameters by 128× for a single attention projection, how QLoRA compresses a 65B frozen base model from 130 GB to 40 GB using NF4 arithmetic, how DPO eliminates the reward model entirely by observing that the optimal RLHF policy has a closed-form expression, how Constitutional AI makes alignment principles auditable and scalable by replacing human annotation with AI critique, and how the entire pipeline from raw weights to a deployed GGUF file can be completed in a few hours on a single GPU for $3–$15 in compute.

What comes next is an open and rapidly moving frontier. Mixture of Experts (MoE) architectures — Mixtral 8x7B, DeepSeek-MoE, Grok-1 — dramatically increase model capacity without proportionally increasing inference cost by routing each token through a small subset of specialized feed-forward "expert" layers rather than all layers. A Mixtral 8x7B model has 47B total parameters but activates only 13B per forward pass; it matches or exceeds LLaMA-2 70B on most benchmarks while requiring 70B/13B = 5.4× less compute at inference. Multimodal models (LLaVA, GPT-4V, Gemini, Idefics) add vision encoders that project image patches into the same embedding space as text tokens using a projection layer trained separately. The language model backbone does not need to be retrained; only the projection layer and a small cross-attention mechanism need fine-tuning. This makes multimodal extension relatively cheap. Reasoning models (OpenAI o1, DeepSeek-R1) insert an extended "chain of thought" generation phase before the final answer. The model is trained — using outcome-based RL rather than SFT — to use this scratchpad for multi-step planning. DeepSeek-R1 (January 2025) demonstrated that this technique can be replicated in open-source at a fraction of the reported cost, using Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) rather than PPO.

The direction the field is moving is unmistakable: smaller models, better data, smarter training. The Phi series (Microsoft) demonstrated that a 3.8B model trained on carefully curated "textbook quality" data can outperform models 10× larger trained on raw web text. Long-context models (Llama-3.1 128K, Gemini 1.5 Pro 1M) enable reasoning over entire codebases, legal documents, and scientific papers in a single forward pass — the context window is becoming the primary unit of "working memory" for LLM applications. Speculative decoding uses a small fast draft model to propose candidate tokens that a large model verifies in parallel, achieving 2–4× inference speedup with mathematically identical outputs. The compute requirements for state-of-the-art performance at the research frontier have grown, but the compute requirements for building a genuinely useful domain-specific assistant have fallen dramatically. A single A100 GPU, a QLoRA fine-tuning run on 2,000 well-curated examples, and two hours of compute is enough to produce a model that outperforms GPT-3 on your specific task. That statement would have been science fiction in 2021. It is a weekend project today.

The six papers that changed everything: Attention Is All You Need (Vaswani et al. 2017) gave us the transformer. Scaling Laws (Kaplan et al. 2020) gave us the compute-optimal recipe. InstructGPT (Ouyang et al. 2022) gave us alignment. LoRA (Hu et al. 2021) gave us accessibility. QLoRA (Dettmers et al. 2023) gave us consumer hardware reach. DPO (Rafailov et al. 2023) gave us simplicity. Every model you interact with today is downstream of these six papers. The next six are probably being written right now.
