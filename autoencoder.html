Autoencoders & Variational Autoencoders

Neural networks that learn compressed representations by reconstructing their own input — and generative extensions that sample new data from structured latent spaces.

Mathematical Foundation

Autoencoders are trained to minimize the difference between input \( \mathbf{x} \) and its reconstruction \( \hat{\mathbf{x}} \). The simplest loss is the Mean Squared Error (MSE):

\[ \mathcal{L}_{\text{recon}} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \| \mathbf{x}_i - \hat{\mathbf{x}}_i \|^2 \]

For binary data (e.g., MNIST pixel intensities normalized to [0, 1]), Binary Cross-Entropy is often preferred:

\[ \mathcal{L}_{\text{BCE}} = -\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \left[ x_i \log(\hat{x}_i) + (1 - x_i) \log(1 - \hat{x}_i) \right] \]

Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) add a regularization term. Instead of encoding to a single point, the encoder outputs parameters \( \boldsymbol{\mu} \) and \( \boldsymbol{\sigma}^2 \) of a Gaussian distribution over the latent space. The total loss combines reconstruction with KL divergence:

\[ \mathcal{L}_{\text{VAE}} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{recon}} + D_{\text{KL}}\left( q(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x}) \;\|\; p(\mathbf{z}) \right) \]

Where the KL term (against a standard normal prior) has a closed-form solution:

\[ D_{\text{KL}} = -\frac{1}{2} \sum_{j=1}^{d} \left( 1 + \log(\sigma_j^2) - \mu_j^2 - \sigma_j^2 \right) \]

The reparameterization trick enables backpropagation through the stochastic sampling layer. Instead of sampling \( \mathbf{z} \sim \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}, \boldsymbol{\sigma}^2) \) directly, we compute:

\[ \mathbf{z} = \boldsymbol{\mu} + \boldsymbol{\sigma} \odot \boldsymbol{\epsilon}, \quad \boldsymbol{\epsilon} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I}) \]

This moves the randomness to an input (\( \boldsymbol{\epsilon} \)) that does not depend on the model parameters, making the gradient with respect to \( \boldsymbol{\mu} \) and \( \boldsymbol{\sigma} \) well-defined.

The Bottleneck Principle

The central insight behind autoencoders is deceptively simple: force a neural network to copy its input through a narrow bottleneck, and it must learn a compressed representation that captures the most salient features of the data.

If the latent dimension equals the input dimension, the network could learn the identity function — trivial and useless. By making the bottleneck smaller than the input, the encoder is forced to prioritize: it must decide which patterns matter and discard noise or redundancy. This is lossy compression learned end-to-end.

The learned representations are often more useful than the raw data for downstream tasks. The bottleneck acts as an information filter, producing features that capture structure, ignore noise, and generalize across examples. This is why autoencoders are used for dimensionality reduction, feature learning, and pretraining.

In VAEs, the bottleneck is further regularized: the latent space must conform to a known distribution (typically a unit Gaussian). This ensures the latent space is smooth and continuous — nearby points decode to similar outputs, and random samples produce coherent outputs. This is what makes VAEs generative.

Key Difference

A vanilla autoencoder maps input to a fixed point in latent space. A VAE maps input to a distribution (mean and variance), then samples from it. This makes the latent space continuous and suitable for generation — you can sample z ~ N(0, I) and decode novel outputs.

Key Concepts

Latent Space

The compressed representation learned by the encoder. Each dimension captures a learned feature. In VAEs, this space is structured to be smooth and continuous, enabling interpolation and generation.

Encoder

Maps high-dimensional input \( \mathbf{x} \) to a low-dimensional latent vector \( \mathbf{z} \). Can be fully connected, convolutional, or any differentiable function. Learns to compress.

Decoder

Maps the latent vector \( \mathbf{z} \) back to the original input space, producing reconstruction \( \hat{\mathbf{x}} \). Mirrors the encoder architecture. Learns to reconstruct.

Bottleneck

The narrowest layer between encoder and decoder. Its dimensionality controls compression: too large and the model memorizes; too small and it loses critical information. Typical: 2–512 dimensions.

Reconstruction Loss

Measures how well the decoder output matches the original input. MSE for continuous data, BCE for binary/normalized data. This is the primary training signal for all autoencoders.

KL Divergence

In VAEs, regularizes the latent distribution toward the prior \( \mathcal{N}(0, I) \). Prevents the encoder from collapsing to delta functions. Balances reconstruction quality vs. latent structure.

Reparameterization Trick

Rewrites sampling as a deterministic function of \( \mu, \sigma \) and external noise \( \epsilon \). Makes the VAE fully differentiable so gradients flow through the sampling step during backpropagation.

Disentangled Representations

When each latent dimension captures an independent factor of variation (e.g., rotation, thickness, style). Achieved via Beta-VAE by upweighting the KL term, encouraging factorial latent distributions.

Code: Vanilla Autoencoder on MNIST

A minimal autoencoder using Keras. The encoder compresses 784-pixel MNIST images to a 32-dimensional bottleneck, and the decoder reconstructs them.

Python — Keras 
import numpy as np
from tensorflow import keras
from tensorflow.keras import layers

# ── Load & preprocess MNIST ──
(x_train, _), (x_test, _) = keras.datasets.mnist.load_data()
x_train = x_train.astype("float32") / 255.0
x_test  = x_test.astype("float32") / 255.0
x_train = x_train.reshape(-1, 784)
x_test  = x_test.reshape(-1, 784)

# ── Encoder ──
encoder_input = layers.Input(shape=(784,), name="encoder_input")
x = layers.Dense(256, activation="relu")(encoder_input)
x = layers.Dense(128, activation="relu")(x)
latent = layers.Dense(32, activation="relu", name="bottleneck")(x)
encoder = keras.Model(encoder_input, latent, name="encoder")

# ── Decoder ──
decoder_input = layers.Input(shape=(32,), name="decoder_input")
x = layers.Dense(128, activation="relu")(decoder_input)
x = layers.Dense(256, activation="relu")(x)
decoder_output = layers.Dense(784, activation="sigmoid")(x)
decoder = keras.Model(decoder_input, decoder_output, name="decoder")

# ── Full Autoencoder ──
autoencoder = keras.Model(
    encoder_input,
    decoder(encoder(encoder_input)),
    name="autoencoder"
)
autoencoder.compile(optimizer="adam", loss="mse")

# ── Train ──
autoencoder.fit(
    x_train, x_train,           # input == target
    epochs=50,
    batch_size=256,
    shuffle=True,
    validation_data=(x_test, x_test)
)

# ── Evaluate reconstruction ──
reconstructed = autoencoder.predict(x_test)
mse = np.mean((x_test - reconstructed) ** 2)
print(f"Test MSE: {mse:.5f}")

# ── Extract latent representations ──
latent_vectors = encoder.predict(x_test)
print(f"Latent shape: {latent_vectors.shape}")  # (10000, 32)

Code: Variational Autoencoder (VAE)

A complete VAE in PyTorch with the reparameterization trick. The encoder outputs mu and log-variance, the sampling layer draws z, and the loss combines BCE reconstruction with KL divergence.

Python — PyTorch 
import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.nn.functional as F
from torch.utils.data import DataLoader
from torchvision import datasets, transforms

# ── Hyperparameters ──
LATENT_DIM = 20
EPOCHS     = 30
BATCH_SIZE = 128
LR         = 1e-3
DEVICE     = torch.device("cuda" if torch.cuda.is_available() else "cpu")


class VAE(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, input_dim=784, hidden=400, latent=20):
        super().__init__()
        # Encoder
        self.fc1    = nn.Linear(input_dim, hidden)
        self.fc_mu  = nn.Linear(hidden, latent)      # mean
        self.fc_var = nn.Linear(hidden, latent)      # log-variance
        # Decoder
        self.fc3 = nn.Linear(latent, hidden)
        self.fc4 = nn.Linear(hidden, input_dim)

    def encode(self, x):
        h = F.relu(self.fc1(x))
        return self.fc_mu(h), self.fc_var(h)

    def reparameterize(self, mu, log_var):
        """Sample z = mu + sigma * epsilon"""
        std = torch.exp(0.5 * log_var)
        eps = torch.randn_like(std)          # epsilon ~ N(0, I)
        return mu + std * eps

    def decode(self, z):
        h = F.relu(self.fc3(z))
        return torch.sigmoid(self.fc4(h))

    def forward(self, x):
        mu, log_var = self.encode(x.view(-1, 784))
        z = self.reparameterize(mu, log_var)
        return self.decode(z), mu, log_var


def vae_loss(recon_x, x, mu, log_var):
    """ELBO = Reconstruction (BCE) + KL Divergence"""
    bce = F.binary_cross_entropy(recon_x, x.view(-1, 784), reduction="sum")
    kl  = -0.5 * torch.sum(1 + log_var - mu.pow(2) - log_var.exp())
    return bce + kl


# ── Data ──
train_loader = DataLoader(
    datasets.MNIST("./data", train=True, download=True,
                   transform=transforms.ToTensor()),
    batch_size=BATCH_SIZE, shuffle=True
)

# ── Train ──
model     = VAE(latent=LATENT_DIM).to(DEVICE)
optimizer = torch.optim.Adam(model.parameters(), lr=LR)

for epoch in range(1, EPOCHS + 1):
    model.train()
    total_loss = 0
    for batch, _ in train_loader:
        batch = batch.to(DEVICE)
        recon, mu, log_var = model(batch)
        loss = vae_loss(recon, batch, mu, log_var)
        optimizer.zero_grad()
        loss.backward()
        optimizer.step()
        total_loss += loss.item()
    print(f"Epoch {epoch}: Loss = {total_loss / len(train_loader.dataset):.2f}")

# ── Generate new samples ──
with torch.no_grad():
    z = torch.randn(16, LATENT_DIM).to(DEVICE)
    samples = model.decode(z).view(-1, 1, 28, 28)
    print(f"Generated {samples.shape[0]} novel images from latent space")

Code: Denoising Autoencoder

A practical denoising autoencoder that learns to remove Gaussian noise from MNIST images. The model receives corrupted input but is trained to reconstruct the clean original — forcing it to learn robust features rather than memorize pixel values.

Python — Keras 
import numpy as np
from tensorflow import keras
from tensorflow.keras import layers

# ── Load & preprocess ──
(x_train, _), (x_test, _) = keras.datasets.mnist.load_data()
x_train = x_train.astype("float32") / 255.0
x_test  = x_test.astype("float32") / 255.0
x_train = x_train.reshape(-1, 784)
x_test  = x_test.reshape(-1, 784)

# ── Add Gaussian noise ──
NOISE_FACTOR = 0.35

def add_noise(data, factor=NOISE_FACTOR):
    noisy = data + factor * np.random.normal(size=data.shape)
    return np.clip(noisy, 0.0, 1.0)

x_train_noisy = add_noise(x_train)
x_test_noisy  = add_noise(x_test)

# ── Denoising Autoencoder ──
inputs  = layers.Input(shape=(784,))
# Encoder
x = layers.Dense(256, activation="relu")(inputs)
x = layers.Dropout(0.3)(x)
x = layers.Dense(128, activation="relu")(x)
encoded = layers.Dense(64, activation="relu", name="bottleneck")(x)
# Decoder
x = layers.Dense(128, activation="relu")(encoded)
x = layers.Dropout(0.3)(x)
x = layers.Dense(256, activation="relu")(x)
decoded = layers.Dense(784, activation="sigmoid")(x)

denoiser = keras.Model(inputs, decoded, name="denoising_ae")
denoiser.compile(optimizer="adam", loss="binary_crossentropy")

# ── Train: noisy input → clean target ──
denoiser.fit(
    x_train_noisy, x_train,     # noisy → clean
    epochs=50,
    batch_size=256,
    shuffle=True,
    validation_data=(x_test_noisy, x_test)
)

# ── Evaluate denoising quality ──
denoised = denoiser.predict(x_test_noisy)
mse_noisy    = np.mean((x_test - x_test_noisy) ** 2)
mse_denoised = np.mean((x_test - denoised) ** 2)
print(f"MSE (noisy input):    {mse_noisy:.5f}")
print(f"MSE (denoised output): {mse_denoised:.5f}")
print(f"Noise reduction:       {(1 - mse_denoised/mse_noisy)*100:.1f}%")

Autoencoder Variants

Vanilla AE

Deterministic encoder-decoder. Learns compressed representations via reconstruction loss alone. Simple, fast, good for dimensionality reduction. No generative capability.

Sparse AE

Adds sparsity penalty (L1 or KL on activations) to the bottleneck. Encourages only a few neurons to activate per input, learning more interpretable features even with overcomplete latent spaces.

Denoising AE

Trained on corrupted inputs to reconstruct clean originals. The corruption (Gaussian noise, masking, dropout) forces the model to learn robust, noise-invariant features rather than the identity.

VAE

Probabilistic encoder outputs distribution parameters (μ, σ). KL divergence regularizes latent space toward N(0, I). Enables generation via sampling. Reparameterization trick enables training.

Beta-VAE

Upweights KL term by factor β > 1. Encourages disentangled latent representations where each dimension controls an independent factor of variation. Trades reconstruction quality for interpretability.

VQ-VAE

Vector Quantized VAE. Replaces continuous latent space with a discrete codebook of learned embeddings. Encoder output is snapped to nearest codebook vector. Enables high-fidelity generation (used in DALL-E).

When to Use / When to Avoid

When to Use

Dimensionality reduction — nonlinear alternative to PCA, especially for image and sequence data

Feature learning / pretraining — extract useful representations for downstream classifiers

Anomaly detection — high reconstruction error flags outliers the model has never seen

Denoising — remove noise, fill missing values, or inpaint corrupted regions

Data generation (VAE) — sample from latent space to produce novel, plausible examples

Compression — learned codecs that adapt to specific data distributions

When to Avoid

Small tabular datasets — PCA or simple feature selection is faster and sufficient

When interpretability is required — latent dimensions are not inherently meaningful (unless disentangled)

High-fidelity image generation — VAEs produce blurry outputs; GANs and diffusion models are superior

Discrete/categorical data — reconstruction loss is poorly defined; consider VQ-VAE or specialized architectures

Real-time inference on edge devices — encoder-decoder adds latency; simpler models may suffice

When labels are abundant — supervised methods usually outperform unsupervised representation learning

Best Practice

  • Start with a vanilla autoencoder to validate your pipeline, then upgrade to VAE or denoising variants
  • Monitor both reconstruction loss and KL divergence separately — if KL collapses to zero, the model ignores the latent space (posterior collapse)
  • Use KL annealing (gradually increase KL weight from 0 to 1 during training) to mitigate posterior collapse
  • Visualize the latent space with t-SNE or UMAP to verify meaningful clustering
  • For convolutional autoencoders, use transposed convolutions or upsampling + conv in the decoder to avoid checkerboard artifacts

Common Pitfalls

  • Posterior collapse — the decoder learns to ignore z and generate from the prior alone. Fix with KL annealing, free bits, or delta-VAE
  • Blurry reconstructions — MSE loss averages over modes. Consider perceptual loss, adversarial loss (VAE-GAN), or VQ-VAE
  • Overfitting — if the bottleneck is too large, the model memorizes rather than generalizes. Add dropout, noise, or reduce latent dim
  • Wrong loss function — using MSE on binary data or BCE on unbounded data produces poor gradients and training instability
